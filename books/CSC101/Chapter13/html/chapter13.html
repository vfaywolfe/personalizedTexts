Tell a Story with Data

Welcome to Data Visualization

Numbers alone rarely tell a story. Imagine trying to spot a pattern in a spreadsheet with 500 rows — your eyes glaze over within seconds. Data visualization transforms those numbers into charts, graphs, and infographics that make patterns instantly visible and insights shareable with anyone, regardless of their technical background.

Key Concepts and Terms

Data Visualization
A graphical representation of data or information — such as charts, graphs, maps, and infographics — that makes patterns and trends easier to see and understand than looking at raw numbers.
Exploratory Visualization
Charts made for yourself during the analysis phase to discover patterns, outliers, and trends in data before drawing final conclusions. These don't need to be polished — their purpose is to help you understand the data.
Explanatory Visualization
Polished charts and graphics made for an audience to clearly communicate a specific finding or conclusion. These need clear titles, labels, and legends.
Scatter Chart
A chart that shows the relationship between two numeric variables. Each point represents one data record, with one variable on the x-axis and another on the y-axis. Best used to explore whether two things are related (correlation).
Line Chart
A chart that shows how data changes over time or across a continuous variable by connecting data points with lines. Best used for trends and changes over time.
Bar Chart
A chart that uses rectangular bars to compare values across distinct categories or groups. One of the most versatile and commonly used chart types.
Infographic
A visual representation that combines graphics, statistics, and brief text to communicate information quickly and clearly to a broad audience. Used in the Communicate Results stage of the data science process.
Data Story
A narrative built around data findings that combines visualizations, statistics, and context to convey a meaningful message to an audience.
Correlation
A statistical relationship between two variables — as one changes, the other tends to change as well. Important: correlation does not mean one variable causes the other (correlation ≠ causation).
Summative Analysis
A comprehensive conclusion that ties together your research question, data collection process, patterns found, and overall findings into a coherent, evidence-based narrative.

The Data Science Process

All semester, you've been following the data science process: you asked a question about a computing innovation, gathered and organized survey data, modeled that data with pivot tables, and analyzed it. Now you've reached the final two stages: Analyze & Synthesize and Communicate Results. Data visualization lives in both.

In the Analyze & Synthesize stage, you create quick, rough charts for yourself — exploratory visualizations — to spot patterns you might miss in a spreadsheet. In the Communicate Results stage, you refine those findings into polished charts and infographics that help others understand your conclusions.

ℹ️ The Data Science Workflow

Ask Questions → Gather & Organize Data → Model → Analyze & Synthesize → Communicate Results

Visualization appears in both the Analyze and Communicate stages — first to help you understand your data, then to share your conclusions with others.

Why Visualize? The Power of a Picture

Good visualizations help people make sense of data sets that are too large to interpret by looking at raw numbers. When you see 30 rows of survey responses in a spreadsheet, it's hard to tell whether older people rated AI differently than younger people. When you plot those same responses on a scatter chart, a trend becomes immediately visible.

Example 13.1.1: Exploratory vs. Explanatory Visualization

Exploratory (for you): You create a quick bar chart in Google Sheets to see which age group gave the highest impact ratings. The chart looks rough — no proper title, axes aren't fully labeled — but you can immediately see that the 18–24 group rated higher than the 50+ group. You now know where to focus your analysis.

Explanatory (for your audience): You refine that same chart with a descriptive title, proper axis labels, a legend, and clean formatting. You add it to your infographic alongside a key statistic and a brief conclusion sentence. Your audience can now understand your finding without any explanation.

Exploring vs. Communicating

Not all visualizations have the same job. When data scientists first receive a dataset, they create quick, rough charts to explore patterns. When they're ready to share findings, they refine those into polished explanatory visuals designed for a specific audience.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Students often think the goal of a chart is to look impressive. The real goal is clarity. A simple, well-labeled bar chart that answers your question beats a flashy 3-D pie chart that confuses everyone. Always ask: Can my audience understand this in 10 seconds?

Lesson 13.2: Choosing the Right Chart Type

Lesson 2 of 6
Learning Objectives: LO13.2.1 — Describe the purpose and best use case for scatter, line, bar, and other common chart types. LO13.2.2 — Match a data question to the most appropriate chart type. LO13.2.3 — Identify the essential elements every chart must include.

Matching Your Chart to Your Question

There are dozens of chart types, and picking the wrong one can actually mislead your audience — even unintentionally. The key is to match your chart type to the kind of comparison or relationship you want to show.

📊
Bar Chart
Comparing values across distinct categories or groups
Example: How do AI impact ratings differ by gender?
📈
Line Chart
Showing trends or change over time (continuous variable)
Example: How have AI adoption rates changed by age group over time?
⚫
Scatter Chart
Exploring the relationship between two numeric variables
Example: Is there a relationship between age and impact rating?
🥧
Pie / Donut Chart
Showing proportions of a whole (use sparingly — max 5 slices)
Example: What percentage of respondents were each gender?
⚠️ Pie Chart Warning

Pie charts become very hard to read with more than 4–5 slices. When in doubt, a bar chart is almost always clearer and allows easier comparison. Ask yourself: Can the reader instantly tell which value is bigger? If not, use a bar chart instead.

Essential Elements Every Chart Must Have

No matter which chart type you use, every effective chart must include:

  • Title — Describes what the chart shows. For your assignment, include your full name in the title.
  • Axis Labels — Clearly label the x-axis (horizontal) and y-axis (vertical), including units where applicable.
  • Legend — Explains colors or symbols when multiple data series are present (e.g., Male vs. Female).
  • Appropriate Scale — Bar chart y-axes should start at zero to avoid visually distorting comparisons.
💡 The One-Sentence Test

Before finalizing a chart, try describing it in one sentence: "This chart shows [X] compared to [Y], and the main finding is [Z]." If you can't fill in all three parts, your chart needs a clearer title or better labels.

Worked Example

Example 13.2.1: Choosing the Right Chart for Survey Data

Scenario: You surveyed 30 people on AI's impact on society. Your data includes each respondent's age (numeric) and their impact rating from 1–5 (numeric). You want to know: Does impact rating change with age?

Step 1 — Identify your variables: Age and impact rating are both continuous numbers — not categories or time-based data.

Step 2 — What relationship are you exploring? You want to see if two numeric variables are related — not compare categories, not track time.

Step 3 — Choose the chart: A Scatter Chart is the right fit. Each point represents one respondent, with Age on the x-axis and Impact Rating on the y-axis.

Step 4 — Add required elements: Title: "The Impact of AI on Society Based on Age — [Your Name]" | x-axis: "Age" | y-axis: "Impact Rating (1–5)" | Legend: colors for Male/Female/Prefer not to say.

Lesson 13.3: Analyzing & Communicating Your Data Story

Reading a Chart: What to Look For

Creating a chart is just the beginning. A data scientist's real skill is reading what the chart says — identifying what the data shows, what it doesn't show, and how to communicate those conclusions honestly and clearly.

Read: What is an Infographic? (PDF)

[Required Reading — Principles of visual communication through infographics]

When analyzing a chart, ask yourself these questions systematically:

  • Overall trend: Is the data generally going up, down, or staying flat?
  • Outliers: Are there any unusual data points that don't fit the pattern?
  • Clusters: Do certain groups of responses appear together?
  • Range: How spread out is the data — is there a wide variation or are values close together?
  • Connection to your question: What does this tell me about my original research question?
Example 13.3.1: Interpreting a Scatter Chart

Scenario: Your scatter chart shows Age on the x-axis and AI Impact Rating (1–5) on the y-axis, with points colored by gender.

Step 1 — Look for a trend: Do the points generally slope upward (older = higher rating) or downward (older = lower rating), or is there no clear pattern?

Step 2 — Check for clusters: Are female respondents clustered at higher or lower ratings than male respondents?

Step 3 — Note any outliers: Is there one 60-year-old who gave a 5/5 when everyone else their age gave 2–3?

Step 4 — Write a careful conclusion: "Based on our survey of 30 respondents aged 20–62, younger respondents (under 30) tended to rate AI's impact more positively (avg. 4.1) compared to older respondents (avg. 3.2), suggesting possible generational differences in AI perception."

⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation

Just because two things appear related in your chart does not mean one causes the other. Your data can show that older people tend to rate AI lower, but it can't tell you why. Always say "the data suggests..." rather than "this proves..."

Summative Analysis: Tying It All Together

A summative analysis combines everything you've learned from your data into a coherent, evidence-based narrative. It answers four key questions — each in 3–5 sentences:

  1. Finalized Question: Revisit "What impact does [TOPIC] have on society?" — did your findings cause you to refine it?
  2. Data Collection Process: Describe how you collected, modeled, and analyzed your data (tools + techniques — Google Forms, Sheets, pivot tables, charts).
  3. Trend or Pattern: What was the most significant pattern in your data? Offer a possible explanation for it.
  4. Summative Conclusion: What does your data analysis say about your computing innovation's impact on society?

Infographic Design Principles

An infographic communicates your entire data story to someone unfamiliar with your project. Good infographics follow these principles:

  • Keep it simple: Include only critical information that supports your conclusion — if you need too much text, visualize it instead.
  • Make it clear: Easy to read at a glance; no cluttered text blocks.
  • In some cases, a simple table is better than a chart — don't force a chart if a table communicates more clearly.
  • Use text strategically: Brief text guides the reader, but visuals do the heavy lifting.
  • Provide enough context: Someone completely unfamiliar with your project should be able to understand your main point.
