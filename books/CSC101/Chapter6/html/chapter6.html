How Search Engines Work and Personalize Results

‚è±Ô∏è 11-13 minutes
Learning Objective (LO6.2): Describe how search engines work and how they tailor results to individual users

Every second, Google processes over 40,000 search queries. But how does it instantly return relevant results from billions of web pages? And why does the search result your friend sees look different from yours?

How Search Engines Index the Web

Search engines use automated programs called crawlers or spiders that:

  1. Visit billions of web pages
  2. Read the content and metadata (information about the page)
  3. Store information in massive databases called indexes
  4. Rank pages based on relevance, quality, and popularity

When you type a search query, you're not actually searching the live web‚Äîyou're searching Google's index of the web.

Personalization: Why Your Results Are Unique

Search engines track information about you to customize results:

  • Search history: What you've searched for before
  • Location: Where you are now
  • Device: Phone, desktop, tablet
  • Time of day: When you're searching
  • Previous clicks: Which results you've clicked on

Video: The Internet - How Search Works (5:12 min)

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Filter Bubbles

Because search engines personalize results based on your history, you might end up seeing mostly content that confirms what you already believe. This is called a filter bubble. It's important to:

  • Search for opposing viewpoints
  • Check multiple sources
  • Be aware that your search results may not represent all perspectives

Example: Two Friends, Different Results

Scenario: You and your friend both search "best restaurants near school" at the same time from the same location.

  • Your results: Show Italian restaurants, vegan cafes, and upscale dining (because that's what you've searched for before)
  • Your friend's results: Show fast food, pizza places, and budget-friendly options (based on their search history)

Why? Google's algorithm personalizes results based on individual user behavior and preferences.

The Physical Infrastructure: Wires, Cables, and WiFi

‚è±Ô∏è 9-11 minutes
Learning Objective (LO6.3): Identify the physical infrastructure that connects the Internet

Right now, thousands of miles of fiber optic cable are running beneath your feet and under the ocean. Satellites are orbiting Earth. Routers and cell towers are sending wireless signals through the air. All of this infrastructure is what makes the Internet possible.

The Three Layers of Internet Infrastructure

Layer 1: Wired Connections

  • Fiber optic cables: Hair-thin glass cables that transmit data as light. They're faster and more reliable than copper wire and can carry information across oceans.
  • Copper cables: Older technology, still used for local connections.
  • Submarine cables: Undersea cables that connect continents.

Layer 2: Wireless Connections

  • WiFi: Wireless signals in homes and public places
  • Cellular towers: 4G and 5G networks for mobile devices
  • Satellites: For remote areas and global coverage

Layer 3: Routing Equipment

  • Routers: Direct data to the correct destination
  • Switches: Connect devices on local networks
  • Data centers: Massive facilities that store servers and keep data flowing

Video: The Internet - Wires, Cables and WiFi (6:40 min)

üåç Global Scale

There are over 1.3 million kilometers of submarine cables on the ocean floor connecting countries across the world. Google, Facebook, and Amazon are now investing in building their own undersea cables to improve connectivity.

Example: Your Data's Journey

Scenario: You send a photo to a friend across the country.

  1. Your WiFi router converts your data into signals
  2. Your Internet Service Provider (ISP) sends it to their equipment
  3. The data travels through fiber optic cables across your state
  4. It reaches regional routing centers that direct it to the right path
  5. It travels through submarine cables if crossing oceans
  6. More routers guide it to your friend's ISP
  7. Finally, it arrives at your friend's device via WiFi or cellular

Lesson 6.4

Packets and Routers: How Data Travels

‚è±Ô∏è 10-12 minutes
Learning Objective (LO6.4): Explain how data travels across networks using packets and routers

Have you ever wondered what happens to your data when you send it over the Internet? It doesn't travel in one continuous stream. Instead, it gets broken into tiny pieces and reassembled at the destination. Here's how.

Packets: Breaking Data into Pieces

When you send any data over the Internet‚Äîa photo, a message, a video stream‚Äîit's broken down into small chunks called packets. Each packet contains:

  • Data payload: The actual content (1-1500 bytes)
  • Source address: Where the packet came from (your IP address)
  • Destination address: Where it's going (recipient's IP address)
  • Sequence number: The packet's order in the message
  • Error checking: Info to detect corruption

Why Break Data into Packets?

Efficiency: Multiple users can share the same network paths simultaneously. If your entire video was sent as one massive stream, it would monopolize the connection.

Reliability: If one packet gets corrupted, only that packet is resent, not the entire message.

Flexibility: Packets can take different routes to the destination. If one path is slow or damaged, another path can be used.

Routers: The Traffic Directors

Routers are devices that read the destination address on each packet and decide which direction to send it. Think of them as postal workers at distribution centers, sorting mail by zip code.

When a packet arrives at a router, it:

  1. Reads the destination IP address
  2. Looks up that address in its routing table
  3. Forwards the packet to the next router in the optimal path
  4. The process repeats until the packet reaches its destination

Watch: The Internet - Packets and Routers

Video: The Internet - Packets and Routers (6:25 min)

üí° Analogy: The Postal System

Think of the Internet like the postal system:

  • Your letter = a packet with your address (source) and recipient's address (destination)
  • Postal workers = routers that read the address and send it in the right direction
  • Mail sorting centers = network switches that direct mail between regions
  • Different mail routes = network paths that can change based on congestion

Example: A Message Takes a Journey

Scenario: You send a text message (200 bytes) from New York to California.

  1. Breaking into packets: Your message might be just one packet (since it's small)
  2. Adding headers: Your IP and the recipient's IP are added
  3. Router 1 (NYC): Reads the destination. "California? Send to Router 2 in Philadelphia"
  4. Router 2 (Philadelphia): "California? That's west. Send to Router 3 in Chicago"
  5. Router 3 (Chicago): "California? Send to Router 4 in Denver"
  6. Router 4 (Denver): "California! Send to the recipient's ISP in Los Angeles"
  7. Final delivery: Your friend's phone receives the message

Timeline: Entire journey takes milliseconds!

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Packets Don't Travel in Order

When you send a video or large file, it might be broken into 1000+ packets. These packets might take different routes and arrive out of order! Your computer's TCP/IP protocol reassembles them in the correct order at the destination. This is why packet loss or routing delays can cause buffering in videos.

Lesson 6.5

IP Addresses and the Domain Name System (DNS)

‚è±Ô∏è 11-13 minutes
Learning Objective (LO6.5): Understand IP addresses and the Domain Name System (DNS)

Every device on the Internet needs a unique address, just like every house needs a street address. This is your IP address. But humans can't remember numbers, so we created domain names. Meet DNS‚Äîthe system that bridges the gap.

Key Terms and Concepts

IP Address (Internet Protocol Address): A unique numerical label assigned to every device connected to the Internet. Example: 192.168.1.1 (IPv4) or 2001:0db8:85a3:0000:0000:8a2e:0370:7334 (IPv6)
IPv4: The older standard using four sets of numbers (0-255). Example: 172.16.0.1. Soon to run out of available addresses as more devices connect.
IPv6: The newer standard with 128 bits of information, allowing for billions of times more addresses than IPv4.
Domain Name: A human-readable name for a website. Example: google.com, wikipedia.org, your-school.edu
DNS (Domain Name System): The system that translates domain names (google.com) into IP addresses (142.250.185.46) so your computer can find the server.

How IP Addresses Work

Think of IP addresses like phone numbers. Each device needs a unique one so others can find it. When you connect to the Internet, your device is assigned an IP address by your Internet Service Provider (ISP) or your local network.

Two types of IP addresses:

  • Public IP address: Your device's address on the global Internet (assigned by ISP)
  • Private IP address: Your device's address on your home or office network (assigned by your router)

The DNS System: From Names to Numbers

Problem: You want to visit google.com, but routers only understand IP addresses, not domain names.

Solution: When you type a URL into your browser:

  1. Your browser sends a query to a DNS resolver (usually provided by your ISP)
  2. The DNS resolver asks root nameservers where to find google.com
  3. It's directed to TLD (Top-Level Domain) servers for .com domains
  4. It's directed to Google's authoritative nameserver
  5. Google's nameserver responds with the IP address: 142.250.185.46
  6. Your browser now knows to connect to 142.250.185.46
  7. Your request travels to Google's servers using routers and packets

üåê Fun Fact: Finding Your IP Address

You can find your public IP address by visiting websites like whatismyipaddress.com or typing "what is my IP" into Google. Your private IP address can usually be found in your device's network settings or by typing ipconfig (Windows) or ifconfig (Mac/Linux) in terminal.

Example: Your Google Sites Website

Scenario: You created a Google Site at https://sites.google.com/view/your-project

When someone visits your site:

  1. They type your domain into their browser
  2. Their browser's DNS resolver looks up the IP address of Google's servers
  3. The DNS returns an IP address (e.g., 142.251.33.14 for Google)
  4. Their browser connects to that IP address
  5. Google's servers recognize the domain and serve your site
  6. Your site appears in their browser

You can buy your own domain name (like yourname-project.com) from domain registrars like GoDaddy, Namecheap, or Google Domains. Once purchased, you point the domain's nameservers to your hosting service (in this case, Google Sites). Then visitors can use your custom domain instead of the long Google Sites URL.

Lesson 6.6

HTTP, HTML, and How Websites Are Delivered

‚è±Ô∏è 10-12 minutes
Learning Objective (LO6.6): Describe how websites are stored and accessed using HTTP and HTML

Your browser is amazing‚Äîit can display images, text, videos, and interactive elements. But how does the server send all that information? Through two key technologies: HTTP and HTML.

Key Terms and Concepts

HTTP (HyperText Transfer Protocol): The protocol (ruleset) that defines how web browsers and servers communicate. It's like a conversation in a language both understand.
HTTPS: HTTP with added security (the "S" stands for Secure). Your data is encrypted so hackers can't intercept it.
HTML (HyperText Markup Language): The code used to structure and display content on web pages. It's what tells your browser "this is a heading," "this is a paragraph," "this is an image."
Web Server: A computer that stores website files and responds to requests from web browsers.
URL (Uniform Resource Locator): The web address you type into your browser. Example: https://www.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet

How HTTP Works: A Request-Response Dance

Step 1: Browser sends an HTTP Request

  • You type a URL or click a link
  • Your browser sends a message saying "Hey, I want the file at /index.html"
  • The request includes your browser type, language preferences, and other info

Step 2: Server processes the request

  • The web server receives the request
  • It finds the requested file
  • It prepares an HTTP Response

Step 3: Server sends an HTTP Response

  • The server sends back the HTML file (and any associated files like CSS for styling, JavaScript for interactivity)
  • It includes a status code: 200 (success), 404 (not found), 500 (server error), etc.

Step 4: Browser renders the page

  • Your browser reads the HTML code
  • It displays the content in a human-readable format

HTML: The Building Blocks of Web Pages

HTML is code that uses "tags" to tell the browser what to display. Here's a simple example:

<html>

My Page



Welcome!


This is a paragraph.

The browser reads this code and displays:

  • A large heading saying "Welcome!"
  • A paragraph of text
  • An image from the file photo.jpg

üí° Tip: Check the Status Code

When you visit a website, the server responds with a status code. Common ones:

  • 200 OK: Success! The page loaded.
  • 301/302: Redirect. "This page moved here."
  • 404 Not Found: Page doesn't exist.
  • 500 Server Error: Something went wrong on the server.

Example: Visiting Your Google Site

Scenario: A friend visits your Google Site at https://sites.google.com/view/your-project

  1. Friend types the URL into their browser
  2. Browser sends an HTTP GET request: "Please get /view/your-project"
  3. The request travels through packets and routers to Google's servers
  4. Google's server finds your site's HTML files
  5. Server sends back an HTTP Response with status "200 OK" and your HTML code
  6. Friend's browser reads the HTML and renders your site
  7. Your banner image, text, videos, and layout all appear in their browser

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Always Use HTTPS

Look for the padlock icon in your browser's address bar. HTTPS (with the S) means your connection is encrypted and secure. HTTP (without the S) sends data unencrypted. Always use HTTPS for sensitive information like passwords, payment info, or personal data. Google automatically provides HTTPS for Google Sites.

Lesson 6.7

The Digital Divide: Access and Equity

‚è±Ô∏è 9-11 minutes
Learning Objective (LO6.7): Recognize the digital divide and its impact on society

You've learned how amazing the Internet is. But not everyone has access to it. While you might take for granted downloading a video or attending a virtual class, billions of people worldwide don't have reliable Internet access. This gap is called the digital divide.

What Is the Digital Divide?

The digital divide is the gap between those with access to the Internet and digital technology and those without. It's more complex than just having or not having internet‚Äîit includes:

  • Access: Do you have Internet available where you live?
  • Affordability: Can you afford a connection and a device?
  • Speed: Is your connection fast enough for today's needs?
  • Reliability: Is the connection stable?
  • Digital literacy: Do you know how to use the technology?

Who Is Affected?

The digital divide isn't just about poor countries. It affects:

  • Rural communities: Limited broadband infrastructure in remote areas
  • Low-income families: Can't afford devices or monthly bills
  • Elderly people: May lack digital literacy or feel intimidated by technology
  • People with disabilities: Lack of accessible, inclusive design
  • Developing nations: Limited infrastructure investment
  • Refugees and displaced people: No stable connectivity

üìä The Numbers

According to the International Telecommunication Union (ITU):

  • Approximately 2.7 billion people worldwide do NOT have Internet access
  • In developed countries, about 87% have Internet access
  • In developing countries, only 45% have Internet access
  • Even in the U.S., about 21 million people lack broadband access

Example: Impact on Education

Scenario 1 (Has access): Sarah lives in a suburb with broadband. During the pandemic, she attended virtual classes, submitted online assignments, and used Khan Academy to supplement her learning. She got to college.

Scenario 2 (No access): Marcus lives in a rural area with no broadband. His school tried to send him home for online classes, but he couldn't participate. He fell behind, couldn't submit assignments, and dropped out.

Same crisis, different outcomes‚Äîbecause of the digital divide.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Divide Widens Inequality

The digital divide amplifies existing inequalities:

  • Economic: Without internet access, people have fewer job opportunities
  • Educational: Students can't access online resources and classes
  • Healthcare: Telemedicine and health information become inaccessible
  • Civic: Can't participate in online government services or voting

üí° What's Being Done?

Organizations and governments are working to bridge the divide:

  • Government programs: Investing in rural broadband infrastructure
  • Public WiFi: Libraries and community centers providing free access
  • Low-cost programs: ISPs offering subsidized internet for low-income families
  • Device donations: Organizations giving refurbished computers to students
  • Digital literacy training: Teaching skills to older adults and new users

Lesson 6.8

Web Accessibility: Designing for Everyone

‚è±Ô∏è 10-12 minutes
Learning Objective (LO6.8): Apply web accessibility principles to digital design

Imagine trying to use a website designed only for people who can see and hear. What if you're blind? Deaf? Colorblind? Have motor disabilities? Web accessibility is about designing websites that everyone can use, regardless of their abilities.

Who Benefits from Accessible Design?

People with disabilities:

  • Vision impairments (blind, low vision, colorblind)
  • Hearing impairments (deaf, hard of hearing)
  • Motor disabilities (difficulty using mouse/keyboard, tremors)
  • Cognitive disabilities (dyslexia, ADHD, autism)

Everyone else:

  • Older adults with declining abilities
  • People with temporary injuries (broken arm)
  • People in environments where access is limited (bright sunlight, noisy offices)

Core Accessibility Principles (WCAG)

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) define four principles. A website should be:

  • Perceivable: Information is accessible to all senses (text for images, captions for videos)
  • Operable: Users can navigate with keyboard (not just mouse)
  • Understandable: Clear language, consistent design, obvious navigation
  • Robust: Works with assistive technologies (screen readers, magnifiers)

Watch: Web Accessibility Perspectives

Video: Web Accessibility Perspectives (7:36 min)

üí° Practical Accessibility Tips

For text and images:

  • Use alt text for all images (describes the image for screen readers)
  • Use clear headings to structure content
  • Use high contrast colors (dark text on light background)

For videos and audio:

  • Include captions for videos (helps deaf users and people in noisy environments)
  • Provide transcripts (helps deaf users and improves SEO)

For navigation:

  • Ensure keyboard navigation (Tab key should work)
  • Use descriptive link text ("Click here" vs. "Learn about digital privacy")
  • Avoid flashing content (can trigger seizures)

Example: Your Google Site - Accessibility Considerations

An accessible Google Site includes:

  • Descriptive headings: "Chapter 6: The Internet" (not just "Section 1")
  • Alt text on images: "Screenshot showing DNS lookup process" (not just leaving it blank)
  • Video captions: Adding captions to embedded videos so deaf users can follow
  • Color + text: Don't rely only on color to convey meaning
  • Readable fonts: Clear, sans-serif fonts that are easy to read
  • Proper contrast: Dark text on light background (not light gray on white)

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Accessibility Matters

Approximately 1 billion people worldwide live with some form of disability. Making the web inaccessible excludes them from:

  • Education and learning opportunities
  • Employment opportunities
  • Social connection and community
  • Access to government services
  • Shopping and commerce

Plus, in many countries, accessibility is a legal requirement. Websites that violate accessibility standards can face lawsuits.
