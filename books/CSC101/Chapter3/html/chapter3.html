What is a Neural Network?

Imagine you're learning to identify dogs. You see thousands of examples: different colors, sizes, breeds. Over time, you recognize patterns‚Äîfloppy ears, tails, fur texture‚Äîand can identify a dog you've never seen before. That's essentially what neural networks do, except they do it with mathematics and data.

Neural networks are computing systems inspired by how biological brains process information. They consist of interconnected nodes (artificial neurons) organized in layers that learn patterns from data.

Neural Network: A computational model consisting of interconnected nodes organized in layers. The network learns by adjusting the strength of connections between nodes based on examples, enabling it to recognize patterns and make predictions.

How Neural Networks Are Structured

Neural networks have three types of layers:

Input Layer: Receives raw data (e.g., pixels in an image, numbers, text features)

Hidden Layers: Perform calculations and extract features. Each layer learns increasingly complex patterns. A network with many hidden layers is called "deep."

Output Layer: Produces the final prediction or classification

Example 1.1.1: A Neural Network Recognizing Handwritten Digits
Input Layer: Receives a 28√ó28 pixel image (784 input nodes, one per pixel)
First Hidden Layer: Detects simple patterns‚Äîedges, curves, lines. It learns: "Are there vertical lines?" "Are there curves?"
Second Hidden Layer: Combines simple features into shapes. It learns: "Do I see a loop?" "Is there a stem?"
Output Layer: Makes the final decision. It outputs 10 predictions (one for each digit 0-9). The strongest output indicates which digit the network believes it's seeing.
Result: "I'm 97% confident this is a 5"
üí° Why Layers Matter

Each layer in a neural network doesn't learn the final answer‚Äîit learns intermediate features that the next layer builds on. This hierarchical learning is what makes neural networks so powerful. Early layers learn simple patterns; deeper layers combine them into increasingly sophisticated understanding.

How Neural Networks Learn: The Training Process

Neural networks learn through a process called training, where they adjust the strength of connections between nodes (called weights) based on examples.

Example 1.1.2: Training a Network Step-by-Step
Step 1 - Forward Pass: Feed an example (a handwritten "5") through the network. It makes a prediction, say "I think this is a 3."
Step 2 - Calculate Error: Compare the prediction to the correct answer. The error is the difference between what the network said (3) and what it should have said (5).
Step 3 - Backward Pass: The error flows backward through the network. Each layer calculates its contribution to the error.
Step 4 - Update Weights: Adjust the connection strengths to reduce the error. Connections that led to wrong predictions are weakened; those that helped are strengthened.
Step 5 - Repeat: This process repeats thousands or millions of times with different examples until the network consistently makes correct predictions.

What Makes Neural Networks Powerful?

Pattern Recognition: Neural networks excel at finding patterns in complex data that humans might miss
Adaptability: They learn from examples rather than following programmed rules, so they adapt to new data
Scalability: They can handle enormous amounts of data and become better with more examples
Hierarchical Learning: Each layer learns different levels of abstraction, from simple to complex
‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Networks Learn Patterns, Not Understanding

Neural networks don't "understand" in the way humans do. They learn statistical patterns in data. If you show a network a handwritten digit that's slightly distorted or unusual, it might get confused. They can also be fooled by adversarial examples‚Äîdeliberately manipulated images that humans recognize easily but networks misclassify.

What is Computer Vision?

Humans instantly recognize a dog, a cat, a stop sign, or a friend's face in a photograph. But how can a computer "see" an image? Computer vision is the field of AI that enables machines to extract meaningful information from images and videos‚Äîto process visual data the way humans process sight.

Computer Vision: A branch of artificial intelligence that develops systems to automatically extract, analyze, and understand visual information from images and videos, enabling machines to make decisions based on what they "see."

How Computers Represent Images

To understand computer vision, we need to know how computers store images. An image is broken down into tiny colored squares called pixels (picture elements). Each pixel has a color represented by numbers‚Äîtypically three numbers for Red, Green, and Blue (RGB).

Example 1.2.1: Understanding Pixels
A 10√ó10 Pixel Image: Contains 100 individual pixels, each storing color information
RGB Color Values (0-255):
  • Red = (255, 0, 0) ‚Äî maximum red, no green, no blue
  • White = (255, 255, 255) ‚Äî all colors at maximum
  • Black = (0, 0, 0) ‚Äî all colors at zero
Scale Up: A 28√ó28 image (small handwritten digit) = 784 input values. A 224√ó224 image (typical for AI) = 50,176 input values. That's a lot of data for a neural network to process!

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

Regular neural networks struggle with images because they treat each pixel independently. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are specifically designed for image processing. They work by examining small regions of the image at a time, learning local patterns like edges, shapes, and textures.

Convolutional Neural Network (CNN): A specialized neural network architecture designed for processing images. It uses convolutional layers that scan images looking for patterns, learning features like edges, shapes, and textures at multiple levels of abstraction.
Example 1.2.2: How CNNs Process an Image
Early Layers (Simple Feature Detection): Scan for basic patterns:
  • Vertical edges (like the side of a cat's ear)
  • Horizontal edges (like a dog's back)
  • Diagonal lines
  • Color gradients
Middle Layers (Pattern Combination): Combine simple patterns into complex shapes:
  • Circles (eyes)
  • Triangles (ears)
  • Curves (face outlines)
Final Layers (Classification): Use all learned features to classify the image:
  • "This is a CAT" (confidence: 95%)
  • "This is a DOG" (confidence: 3%)
  • "This is a BIRD" (confidence: 2%)
‚ÑπÔ∏è Why CNNs are Efficient

CNNs recognize that images have spatial structure. A detected edge in the top-left corner has similar meaning to an edge in the bottom-right corner. By learning to detect patterns wherever they appear, CNNs are much more efficient than standard neural networks and transfer what they learn more effectively.

The Golden Rule: Garbage In, Garbage Out

No matter how sophisticated your neural network, no matter how many layers or nodes it has, there's one absolute truth: A model is only as good as the data it learns from.

‚ö†Ô∏è Fundamental Principle: GIGO

"Garbage In, Garbage Out" is the core principle of machine learning. Poor quality, biased, or incomplete training data produces poor and biased predictions‚Äîno algorithm can overcome this fundamental limitation.

Training Data: The collection of examples used to teach a machine learning model. Each example includes inputs and their correct outputs (labels). The quality, size, and diversity of this data directly determines model performance.

What Makes Good Training Data?

1. Size: More examples generally lead to better learning. With 10 examples, a model memorizes. With thousands, it learns genuine patterns. There are diminishing returns, but more data is usually helpful.
2. Diversity: Examples should represent the variety of real-world scenarios. A model trained only on sunny photos fails on cloudy days. Diverse data teaches general patterns, not memorized specifics.
3. Accuracy of Labels: Correct answers are essential. Mislabeled data teaches the model incorrect patterns. Even 10% mislabeling significantly impacts performance.
4. Balance: Similar numbers of each category prevent bias. If 99% of examples are spam and 1% are legitimate, the model learns to classify everything as spam.
5. Relevance: Training data should match real-world deployment conditions. Models trained on studio photos fail on phone camera photos.

How Dataset Size Affects Performance

Example 1.3.1: The Impact of Dataset Size
Small Dataset (10 examples per category):
  • Model learns few patterns; may memorize specific examples
  • Poor generalization to new data
  • Typical accuracy: 60-70%
Medium Dataset (1,000 examples per category):
  • Model learns solid, generalizable patterns
  • Better performance on new data
  • Typical accuracy: 85-90%
Large Dataset (100,000+ examples per category):
  • Model learns rich, nuanced patterns
  • Excellent generalization
  • Typical accuracy: 95%+

How Dataset Diversity Affects Performance

Example 1.3.2: Training a Cat Classifier
Homogeneous Dataset (only orange tabbies):
  • Model learns: "Cats are orange with stripes"
  • When shown a black cat ‚Üí "Not a cat!" (Wrong!)
  • When shown a white fluffy cat ‚Üí "Not a cat!" (Wrong!)
  • Model has memorized a specific type, not the concept "cat"
Diverse Dataset (orange, black, white, fluffy, short-haired, young, old):
  • Model learns: "Cats have whiskers, pointy ears, four legs, a tail..."
  • Successfully identifies any cat, regardless of color or fur type
  • Model learns the general concept of "cat"
üí° Diversity = Generalization

Diverse training data teaches models the essential characteristics of what they're learning to recognize, rather than surface-level patterns specific to the training set. This is why real-world ML projects invest heavily in data collection and curation.

Lesson 1.4: AI Bias and Its Consequences

‚è±Ô∏è 14-15 minutes (includes case study analysis)

  • LO1.4.2: Identify types of bias including training data bias, language bias, and selection bias

What is AI Bias?

AI systems can make unfair or inaccurate predictions affecting certain groups more than others. AI bias refers to systematic errors or unfair treatment embedded in machine learning systems‚Äîusually unintentionally.

AI Bias: Systematic errors in machine learning predictions or decisions that disproportionately affect certain groups or demographics. Bias typically originates from biased training data, incomplete representation, or flawed assumptions in system design.
‚ö†Ô∏è Bias ‚â† Intentional Discrimination

Most AI bias isn't deliberate. It emerges from historical patterns in data, underrepresented groups in training data, or unconscious assumptions. However, the impact is the same: unfair and inaccurate outcomes for affected groups. Impact matters more than intent.

Types of AI Bias

1. Training Data Bias

Historical inequities get baked into training data, and machine learning systems learn and amplify them.

Example 1.4.1: Hiring Algorithm Bias
The Problem: A company trains a hiring algorithm on 20 years of historical data. In that data, 90% of executives were men (reflecting the male-dominated tech industry of that era).
What the Algorithm Learns: The system identifies patterns: "Executives have these characteristics... and those characteristics are associated with men." It learns to promote men.
Real Impact: The algorithm systematically rates female candidates lower. Amazon's hiring tool had exactly this problem and was abandoned.
The Lesson: Historical bias in data gets learned and automated by ML systems, perpetuating and amplifying historical discrimination.

2. Selection Bias

When training data comes from limited sources or non-representative populations, the model fails for underrepresented groups.

Example 1.4.2: Face Recognition Bias
The Problem: A face recognition system is trained on millions of photos from social media and phone cameras. However, 80% of users are from wealthy countries and 90% of photos show lighter skin tones.
Result: The model has minimal exposure to darker skin tones, leading to poor recognition accuracy for people with darker skin.
Documented Reality: Major systems from Google, Microsoft, and IBM had error rates of 20-35% for darker-skinned women vs. 1-2% for lighter-skinned men.
Real-World Harm: Used in law enforcement, these systems are more likely to misidentify and wrongly arrest people with darker skin.

3. Language and Cultural Bias

Language models trained on text from certain regions or cultures develop biased responses to others.

Example 1.4.3: Language Model Bias
The Problem: An AI chatbot is trained primarily on English text from Western sources (books, websites, forums from the US, UK, etc.).
What Happens: The system understands Western contexts well but struggles with non-Western contexts and references. It may provide culturally inappropriate or irrelevant advice.
Example: A student from India asking about university systems gets advice tailored to US universities. A user asking about traditional medicines is dismissed as unscientific.
‚ÑπÔ∏è The Feedback Loop Problem

AI bias creates dangerous feedback loops. If a hiring algorithm rejects more female candidates, those women don't get hired. Future training data shows even fewer women in those roles. The next model is even more biased. Without intervention, bias gets worse over time.

Real-World Consequences of AI Bias

üö® Criminal Justice

COMPAS, a risk assessment algorithm used in criminal courts, was found to have racial bias. It incorrectly classified Black defendants as high-risk at nearly twice the rate of white defendants. This directly affects sentencing decisions and affects whether someone goes to prison.

üè• Healthcare

An algorithm determining who receives expensive healthcare programs used race as a proxy for healthcare needs, systematically denying care to Black patients with the same medical conditions as white patients.

üí∞ Lending and Finance

Algorithms have denied loans or offered worse terms to minority applicants with identical credit profiles to majority applicants who were approved.

üíº Employment

Automated hiring systems have discriminated against women, people with disabilities, and older workers based on biased historical data.

Strategies for Addressing AI Bias

1. Collect Diverse, Representative Data

Intentionally collect training data that includes adequate representation of all groups the system will affect. This requires effort but is foundational.

2. Audit and Test for Bias

Before deploying an AI system, rigorously test it across different demographic groups. Look for performance disparities. If the system performs well for some groups but poorly for others, bias exists.

3. Define and Measure Fairness

Different contexts require different fairness definitions:

  • Equal Accuracy: System performs equally well for all groups
  • Equal False Positive Rate: Incorrect positive predictions occur at similar rates
  • Equal Opportunity: Qualified individuals from all groups have equal chances

4. Involve Affected Communities

Include people from affected communities in design, development, and testing. They identify potential harms that others might miss.

5. Transparency and Explainability

Make AI systems transparent about how they reach decisions. This allows bias detection and provides affected individuals with explanations for decisions that affect them.

üí° Ongoing Process

Addressing AI bias is not a one-time fix. It requires continuous monitoring, regular audits, and willingness to adjust systems when new biases are discovered. As society and data change, new biases may emerge.

How Quick, Draw! Works

Quick, Draw! is an interactive game where you doodle and a neural network tries to guess what you've drawn. Behind the scenes, several important things are happening:

Step 1 - Your Drawing: You draw on a digital canvas. The system records your drawing as a series of pixels and coordinates.

Step 2 - Neural Network Processes: A convolutional neural network (similar to what you learned in Lesson 1.2) processes your drawing in real-time, looking for patterns.

Step 3 - Prediction: The network outputs confidence scores for different categories: Is it a cat? A tree? A cloud? It guesses based on learned patterns.

Step 4 - Learning: Your drawing gets added to the world's largest public doodle dataset (if you consent), helping the neural network learn from your contribution.

Collective Learning: The process where machine learning systems improve by learning from data contributed by millions of people. Your individual data point is tiny, but millions of similar points create powerful patterns for AI to learn.

The Power of Collective Data

Example 1.5.1: How Millions of Doodles Improve AI
Early Stage: The neural network sees 100 examples of "trees" drawn by 100 different people. Trees have variation: some are triangles, some are circular, some are realistic, some are abstract.
Growing Dataset: After 10,000 doodles of trees, the network has learned many patterns: "Trees often have a trunk at the bottom," "They might be green," "They have a roughly tree-shaped outline," etc.
Massive Dataset: After millions of doodles, the network has learned incredibly nuanced patterns about what makes a "tree," accounting for cultural differences, artistic styles, and individual variations.
Result: The network can recognize your unique tree drawing even though it may never have seen anything exactly like it before.

Core Concepts You've Learned

  • Neural Networks Learn Patterns: Neural networks, inspired by biological brains, learn by adjusting connections between nodes. They identify patterns in training data and apply those patterns to make predictions on new data.
