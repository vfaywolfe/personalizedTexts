Key Concepts and Terms

Computing Innovation
The specific AI-powered technology you are researching and analyzing — for example, AI in healthcare diagnostics, AI in creative writing, AI in agriculture, AI in education, etc.
Critical Analysis
Examining a topic from multiple perspectives with evidence — not just describing what it is, but evaluating its effects, risks, benefits, and broader implications for people and society. The strongest final projects go beyond "AI is used in X" to examine why it matters and who is affected.
Digital Artifact
A product created using digital tools. In this course, your digital artifacts include: AI-generated images, data visualizations, infographics, embedded videos, survey results, and your Google Site.
AI Ethics
Principles that guide responsible and fair use of AI tools. For this project, you must address four principles: Transparency, Attribution, Responsibility, and Authenticity.
Transparency (AI Ethics)
Being open and honest about when and how you used AI tools in your research and content creation process.
Attribution (AI Ethics)
Giving proper credit for any AI-generated content or AI-assisted work included in your project.
Responsibility (AI Ethics)
Verifying the accuracy of AI-generated information and taking ownership of your final work — you are responsible for everything in your project, even if AI helped create it.
Authenticity (AI Ethics)
Ensuring your project reflects your own genuine understanding and analysis, even when using AI assistance. The thinking must be yours.

Critical Analysis vs. Description — Know the Difference

The most important distinction in this project is between describing AI and critically analyzing its impact. Responses that only describe what AI is will not demonstrate the depth of thinking required.

Example 14.1.1: Descriptive vs. Critical Response

Weak (descriptive):
"AI is used in healthcare. It helps doctors. There are some risks."

Strong (critical analysis):
"AI diagnostic tools can analyze medical imaging with accuracy comparable to experienced radiologists, reducing diagnostic delays in under-resourced hospitals that lack specialists (Smith, 2023). However, a 2022 study found several AI diagnostic tools perform significantly worse on images from patients of color, raising serious equity concerns. Responsible deployment in healthcare therefore requires diverse training data, independent audits, and transparent reporting of performance gaps across demographic groups."

Notice: specific technology → specific benefit → specific concern → specific recommendation, all supported by research.

⚠️ Key Reminder

Successful final projects exhibit evidence of critical thinking supported by research — not just descriptive or opinion-based writing. You need at least three cited, reliable sources on your References page.

The Presentation Format

Rather than creating a separate slide deck, you will take your audience on a live tour of your website — visiting each page and explaining your research, analysis, and conclusions out loud in your own words.

Your instructor will provide guidance on the format: in-person presentations or recorded video. For online sections, you will record a video using a screen-recording tool.

⚠️ Don't Just Read the Website

A common mistake is reading the website word-for-word. Instead, use brief speaker notes to summarize and expand on key points. The website is your visual aid — you are the narrator who brings it to life with insight and explanation.

Presentation Requirements

  • Length: 5–8 minutes
  • Must address all prompts A–F by visiting each page of your website
  • Must briefly describe how you created your Google Site (stored, edited, and accessed on the web)
  • Must briefly describe the graphics and videos used (tools and techniques)
  • On the Survey page, explain how you used Google Forms and Google Sheets to collect and analyze data
  • On the References page, follow a link to your most useful source to show the audience where you found your information

Recommended Presentation Structure

Example 14.3.1: How to Structure Your 5–8 Minute Presentation

Opening (~30 sec): Briefly introduce your topic and why you chose it.

Google Sites overview (~30 sec): Explain that your site is stored on Google's cloud servers, edited via the Sites browser editor, and accessed via a public URL after publishing.

Graphics & video (~30 sec): Briefly describe your AI-generated image, any videos, and your infographic — the tools used to create or find them.

Home page — Prompts A & B (~1.5 min): State each prompt question, then summarize your key points. Don't read verbatim.

Impact page — Prompts C, D & E (~2.5 min): State each prompt, summarize your key findings and analysis. Mention your infographic.

Survey page (~1 min): Explain how you used Google Forms and Sheets to collect and analyze data. Show your chart and describe what it reveals.

References page — Prompt F (~1 min): Summarize your AI ethics statement. Navigate to your most useful source and show the audience where you got your information.

How Google Sites Works

Your presentation must briefly explain how your Google Site is stored, edited, and accessed. Here is a quick reference:

  • Stored: Your site lives on Google's cloud servers — no software to install, no files that can be lost locally.
  • Edited: Via the Google Sites editor in your browser — a drag-and-drop interface that saves changes in real time.
  • Accessed: Anyone with the link can view it via a unique URL on the web (but only after you click Publish).
  • Published vs. Draft: You must click "Publish" for others to see your changes. Changes in draft mode are not visible to visitors.
ℹ️ Don't Forget to Publish

After making all your final changes to your Google Site, always click the Publish button in the top-right corner. Check the live URL in a new browser tab (or incognito window) to confirm everything looks correct before submitting the link.

Recording Your Video (Video Sections)

For video presentations, screen-record yourself navigating your website while speaking through each section. Recommended tools:

  • ClipChamp — Free version produces 480p video (sufficient for this assignment)
  • iMovie or ShotCut — Other free video recording/editing options

Upload your completed video to Google Drive or YouTube to obtain a shareable URL for submission.

💡 Practice First

Do a full practice run before recording. If your practice run goes over 8 minutes, cut content from your explanations. If it goes under 5 minutes, add more depth and analysis to your discussion of the prompts — don't just rush through them.
