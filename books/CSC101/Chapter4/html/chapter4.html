Key Concepts & Terms

Large Language Model (LLM)
An AI system trained on massive amounts of text data that learns patterns in language and can generate human-like responses. Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini.
Generative AI
AI systems designed to create or generate new content‚Äîlike text, images, or code‚Äîbased on patterns learned from training data.
Training Data
The vast collection of text, images, or other information used to teach an AI system patterns and relationships. LLMs are typically trained on billions of words from books, websites, and other sources.
Parameters
The "learned settings" in an AI model‚Äîthink of them like the weights and connections in an artificial brain. Larger models have more parameters (billions or trillions).

What Is a Large Language Model?

Imagine you've read every book in a massive library‚Äîhundreds of billions of words. Over time, you start noticing patterns: when people use certain words, what words tend to come next. You learn that after "Once upon a," the word "time" usually follows. After "What is 2 + 2?", people usually respond with "4."

That's essentially what a large language model (LLM) does. It's a type of artificial intelligence that learns patterns from enormous amounts of text data. When you ask it a question, it uses these learned patterns to predict the most likely next word, then the word after that, and so on‚Äîbuilding a complete response.

LLMs are not: They don't search the internet in real-time (unless explicitly designed to), they don't understand language the way humans do with true comprehension, and they don't "think" or have consciousness. They're pattern-matching machines trained on text.

How LLMs Differ from Traditional Programs

Traditional computer programs are like recipe books‚Äîyou write explicit instructions: "IF the user enters their age, THEN calculate their birth year." Every step is programmed.

LLMs are different. Instead of following explicit rules, they work by recognizing patterns. No programmer wrote the rule "after 'What is 2+2?', respond with '4'". The model learned this pattern from reading countless examples in its training data.

Traditional Program vs. LLM
Traditional Program:
IF user_age < 18 THEN show("You are a minor")
LLM Approach: "Based on patterns from billions of texts, when someone tells me their age, here are the most likely appropriate responses..."

Examples of LLMs You Might Know

  • ChatGPT (by OpenAI) - The popular AI chatbot
  • Claude (by Anthropic) - Known for detailed, thoughtful responses
  • Google Gemini (formerly Bard) - Google's AI assistant
  • Microsoft Copilot - AI assistant built into Windows and Microsoft products
  • Auto-complete on your phone - Uses similar pattern-matching technology!

When you're texting and your phone suggests the next word you might type, that's a simple version of the same technology behind ChatGPT‚Äîjust much, much smaller.

How Chatbots Think: Neural Networks & Training

Key Concepts & Terms

Neural Network
A computational system inspired by biological brains, made of interconnected nodes (artificial neurons) that process information and learn patterns. These networks are organized in layers.
Training
The process of feeding a neural network massive amounts of data and adjusting its internal parameters so it learns to recognize patterns and make accurate predictions.
Tokens
Small pieces of text (words or parts of words) that an LLM breaks language into for processing. When ChatGPT responds, it's generating tokens one at a time.

The Brain Behind the Bot

Think of a neural network like a vast network of interconnected decisions. When you give it an input (like a question), that input passes through thousands or millions of these connections, each one adjusting the signal slightly. By the time the information reaches the output, the network has transformed your input into a response.

During training, the network learns which connections to strengthen and which to weaken by processing billions of examples. Over time, it develops the ability to predict what should come next.

An Analogy: Imagine learning a new language by reading billions of books. You don't memorize every sentence, but over time, you develop an intuition for which words and phrases "sound right" together. That's similar to how neural networks learn.

How Training Works

When Anthropic trained Claude or OpenAI trained GPT, they didn't write out rules for every possible scenario. Instead:

  1. Feed in massive training data: Billions of words from books, websites, and other sources
  2. Make predictions: The network predicts what word should come next
  3. Check if it's right: Compare the prediction to the actual word in the training data
  4. Adjust: If wrong, adjust the network's internal parameters to do better next time
  5. Repeat: Do this billions of times until the model gets very good at predictions

Training Data Matters A Lot

The quality and nature of training data directly shapes what an AI model will do. This is crucial:

  • If trained mostly on formal academic writing, it will tend to respond formally
  • If training data contains biases or inaccuracies, the model will reproduce those
  • If it never saw examples of a certain topic, it will struggle with that topic

This is why it's critical to know when to trust AI and when to verify its outputs. The model can only be as good as the patterns it learned from its training data.

Key Concepts & Terms

Multimodal AI
AI systems that can process multiple types of input‚Äîtext, images, audio, video‚Äîall in one model. These models can analyze images AND respond with text.
Computer Vision
A field of AI focused on teaching computers to "see" and understand images and videos by identifying objects, patterns, text, and other visual elements.
PII (Personally Identifiable Information)
Information that can be used to identify a specific individual, like names, addresses, phone numbers, faces, or locations. Sharing images containing PII with AI tools raises privacy concerns.

What is Multimodal AI?

Earlier, we talked about LLMs that process text. But modern AI has evolved. Multimodal AI models can process multiple types of input‚Äînot just words, but images, audio, and even video.

ChatGPT (with vision), Claude, and Google Gemini can all analyze images you upload and describe what they see. They're trained on both text AND images, so they can understand visual information and translate it into words.

Real-world examples of multimodal AI:

  • Image recognition systems that identify objects or faces
  • Medical AI that analyzes X-rays and MRI scans
  • Self-driving car AI that processes video and lidar data
  • Social media AI that suggests hashtags based on images you post

Here's something important to consider: what if an image contains information about YOU?

  • Data permanence: Once you upload an image to an AI service, you may lose control of it

Never upload images to AI tools that contain: Your face, identifying documents (ID, passport), credit cards, or sensitive information about others. Always check the privacy policy of the AI tool first!

Key Concepts & Terms

Prompt Context
The background information and framing you provide in your prompt that helps shape how the AI interprets your request. Context includes your role, the situation, and what you're trying to accomplish.
Prompt Engineering
The practice of carefully crafting prompts to get better responses from AI. It's like learning the "language" that helps AI understand what you really want.

The Power of Context

Here's a fascinating truth: the same image can generate completely different responses from AI depending on how you ask about it. The context you provide shapes how the AI interprets what it sees.

Example: Same Image, Different Contexts
Scenario 1 - Food Blogger:
"I'm a food blogger. Help me write a caption for this image."
AI might respond: "Golden, crispy-looking dish with fresh herbs... perfect for Instagram! Try: 'Just created this masterpiece‚Äîtap to get the recipe!'"
Scenario 2 - Student:
"I'm studying nutrition. What does this image tell us about healthy eating habits?"
AI might respond: "This dish shows good color variety, suggesting different nutrients. The portion size appears reasonable, and visible vegetables indicate..."

"I'm planning a vacation. What recommendations do you have based on this image?"
AI might respond: "This looks like a Mediterranean or local cuisine dish! If you're interested in culinary tourism, consider destinations known for this type of food..."

Notice what's happening: The AI isn't seeing different things in the image. What changes is how it interprets and emphasizes different aspects based on the context YOU provide.

How AI Differs from Human Perception

Humans don't just observe images‚Äîwe interpret them through our experiences, emotions, and goals. AI works differently:

  • Humans: "I see a pizza. I'm hungry. This makes me want Italian food."
  • AI: "I recognize patterns that match 'pizza.' Based on training data linking images to descriptions, here are likely relevant features..."

Key insight: AI doesn't experience hunger or desire. It processes patterns. Your prompt tells it which patterns are most relevant to emphasize.

Key Concepts & Terms

Few-Shot Prompting
A technique where you provide a few examples in your prompt to show AI what kind of output you want. This significantly improves response quality by giving the model a pattern to follow.
Zero-Shot vs. Few-Shot
Zero-shot: asking AI without any examples. Few-shot: providing 1-3 examples to demonstrate the pattern you want. Few-shot prompts typically generate better results.

Why Examples Work

Remember how LLMs learn by recognizing patterns? When you provide examples in your prompt, you're showing the model a pattern to follow. This is incredibly powerful.

Think of it this way: If you told someone "Give me advice about my career," they might give generic tips. But if you said "My friend studied physics and became an engineer. My other friend studied history and became a lawyer. I'm studying computer science‚Äîwhat should I do?" You've given examples of the pattern you're asking about, and the response will be more targeted.

The Three Levels of Prompting

Career Path Prompting Challenge
Level 1: Basic Prompting (No Examples)
"I'm interested in a career in [your field]. What advice do you have?"
What happens: AI gives general, broad advice. Generic career tips.
Level 2: Add One Example
"My friend studied graphic design and now works at a creative agency creating logos for clients. I'm interested in a career in [your field]. What similar advice do you have?"
What happens: AI recognizes the pattern. Response becomes more specific and tailored to your situation.
Level 3: Provide Multiple Examples
"I've noticed a pattern: my friend Sarah studied software development, built a portfolio on GitHub, and landed a job at a tech startup. My friend Mike studied data science, completed Kaggle competitions, and got hired at a Fortune 500 company. Both started learning through free online courses. I'm interested in [your field]. Based on this pattern, what specific advice do you have?"
What happens: Response is highly specific, detailed, and directly aligned with the pattern you've demonstrated.

Pro tip: The more examples you provide, the better the AI understands what you want. But 2-3 examples are usually enough. More than that, and you might get repetitive or overwhelming output.

Connecting to LLM Training

Why do examples work so well? Remember from Lesson 4.2: LLMs learn patterns during training. When you provide examples in your prompt, you're essentially doing a tiny version of training in real-time. You're showing the model the pattern you want it to recognize and follow.

This is powerful because it shows that the same principles that make AI training work also make prompting work. More examples = clearer pattern = better results.

AI's Wild Imagination: Hallucinations and False Information

‚è±Ô∏è 10-12 minutes
Lesson 4.7

  • LO4.7.2: Recognize real-world consequences of AI errors
  • LO4.7.3: Develop critical thinking skills for evaluating AI output

Key Concepts & Terms

Hallucination
When an AI confidently generates false, made-up, or nonsensical information. The model sounds confident even though it's completely wrong. This happens because the AI is predicting what text "should" come next, not verifying facts.
Confabulation
Creating false memories or information to fill gaps. AI does this too‚Äîwhen asked about something outside its knowledge, it may "make up" plausible-sounding but false information rather than admitting it doesn't know.

Why Hallucinations Happen

Remember: LLMs work by predicting the next word based on patterns. They don't have a fact-checking system built in. If you ask about something obscure or outside its training data, it might generate something that "sounds right" based on patterns, but is completely false.

Example of a Hallucination
You ask:
"Who is the President of Papua New Guinea?"
AI might respond:
"John Mitchell has served as President since 2019..."
Problem: This sounds plausible. But if you verify it, you'll find the information is wrong. AI made it up based on pattern prediction.

The danger: AI sounds confident when it halluccinates. It won't say "I'm not sure." This makes hallucinations particularly dangerous because they're so convincing.

Real-World Case Studies

Case Study 1: Lawyers in Trouble
What happened: A law firm used ChatGPT to write a legal brief. ChatGPT generated citations to fake cases‚Äîmade-up cases that sounded real.
The consequence: The lawyers submitted these fake citations to a judge. The judge fined them \$5,000 and questioned their use of AI.
The lesson: Never use AI to generate citations, statistics, or references without verifying them independently. AI can hallucinate entire sources.
Case Study 2: Airline Customer Service
What happened: An airline used an AI chatbot to handle customer service. A customer asked about a specific promotion. The chatbot hallucinated a promotion that didn't exist.
The consequence: The customer tried to use the fake promotion. When it failed, they sued the airline. The airline was held liable for the AI chatbot's false information.
The lesson: AI should only provide information you can verify. For customer-facing AI, facts must be double-checked before being shared.
Case Study 3: Medical Transcription
What happened: An AI transcription tool was used in hospitals to transcribe doctor-patient conversations. The AI "hallucinated" medical interactions that never happened.
The consequence: False medical records could lead to misdiagnosis or wrong treatment. This is a serious patient safety issue.
The lesson: In high-stakes fields like medicine, AI output MUST be reviewed by humans before being used. Never trust AI to generate medical or legal information without verification.

How to protect yourself: Always verify AI output, especially for facts, citations, statistics, or anything that matters. Use AI as a starting point, but don't trust it as a final source.

How to Catch Hallucinations

  • Verify citations: If AI mentions a study, book, or case, look it up
  • Cross-reference facts: Check important facts against multiple reliable sources
  • Watch for vague language: "According to some sources" often precedes hallucinations
  • Question specific details: Ask AI to cite its sources for claims
  • Use your judgment: If something sounds suspicious, investigate before trusting it

Key Concepts & Terms

Task
The specific, actionable instruction in your prompt. A clear task tells AI exactly what you want it to do.

Examples
