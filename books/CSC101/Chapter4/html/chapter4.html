What Large Language Models Really Are

A Large Language Model (LLM) is an AI system trained on billions of words of text to recognize and generate language patterns. The word "large" refers to its enormous size‚ÄîGPT-4 has over 100 billion parameters.

üéØ Key Insight: Pattern Prediction, Not Understanding

LLMs don't "understand" language like you do. They predict the most likely next word based on statistical patterns. This prediction is sophisticated enough to sound remarkably human‚Äîbut it's fundamentally pattern-matching, not reasoning.

How LLMs Generate Text: Three Phases of Training

Phase 1: Initial Training on Massive Text Data

The model reads billions of words. It learns which words tend to appear together and what sequences are likely. This teaches it to predict: given starting text, what word should come next?

Phase 2: Instruction Fine-Tuning

The model is trained on question-and-answer pairs where experts wrote high-quality responses. This teaches it to follow instructions and respond helpfully to direct questions.

Phase 3: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

Human trainers rate different AI responses. The model learns to optimize for responses humans rate as helpful, harmless, and honest.

‚úì

Pro Tip: Understanding RLHF

The politeness, clarifying questions, and conversational tone you experience with ChatGPT aren't accidental. They're learned preferences from human trainers.

The Token Prediction Process

When you type a prompt, the LLM generates text through this process:

Step What Happens
1. Tokenization Your prompt is broken into small pieces called tokens
2. Embedding Each token is converted to numerical data representing meaning
3. Processing Tokens flow through neural layers that analyze relationships
4. Prediction Model calculates probabilities for every possible next token
5. Selection One token is selected (usually most likely, with some randomness)
6. Repetition Steps 3-5 repeat, generating text one piece at a time
‚ö†Ô∏è

Important: LLMs Have No Access to Facts

LLMs don't check predictions against reality. They have no internet access or fact databases. They simply predict likely text. This is why they can confidently generate completely false information.

Key Terms

Neural Network: A computer system inspired by brains, with interconnected layers of artificial "neurons" that process information.
Token: A small unit of text‚Äîusually a word or part of a word. LLMs work one token at a time, generating ~100-150 tokens per second.
Parameter: An adjustable value in a neural network learned from training data. More parameters allow learning more patterns.

What is Multimodal AI?

A multimodal AI system can process multiple types of input: text, images, audio, and video. Examples include ChatGPT with Vision, Google Gemini, and Claude 3.

üí°

Why Multimodal Matters

Multimodal AI dramatically expands capabilities. You can upload a photo, screenshot, chart, or diagram. The AI analyzes the visual information and integrates it with language capabilities.

How AI "Sees" Images

AI doesn't see images like you do. When an image is uploaded, here's what happens:

The AI Vision Process

  1. Image Conversion: The image is converted into numerical data (pixel values and relationships)
  2. Low-Level Features: Neural layers detect basic patterns: edges, colors, textures
  3. Mid-Level Features: Deeper layers recognize complex patterns: objects, faces, text
  4. High-Level Understanding: Even deeper layers connect patterns to concepts: "This is a dog"
  5. Language Generation: Visual understanding is converted to words
‚ö†Ô∏è

Privacy Alert: Before Uploading Images

Think carefully about what information an image contains. AI can extract: location (landmarks, signs), identity (faces), background (home, workplace). Always check privacy policies.

Context Changes Everything

The same image produces completely different AI responses depending on your prompt. The AI interprets through the lens you provide.

üéØ The Power of Context

Imagine uploading a pizza photo to three different prompts:

  • Context 1 (Food blogger): AI focuses on ingredients, presentation, appetizing qualities
  • Context 2 (Student): AI discusses concentration, nutrition, study breaks
  • Context 3 (Travel planner): AI suggests Italian restaurants, culinary tourism

Same image. Completely different responses. Output depends on context you provide.

üì∫ 5 Essential Elements of Effective AI Prompts
6:12 minutes

Why Prompt Structure Matters

A vague prompt gets vague results. A well-structured prompt gets targeted results. The difference is dramatic.

Weak vs. Strong Prompt

Weak: "Tell me about climate change"

Problem: AI doesn't know angle, level of detail, audience, or format. Default: generic response.

Strong: "I'm a high school science teacher preparing a 15-minute lecture for 9th graders. Summarize the three main causes of climate change in bullet points. Use simple language with one real-world example per cause. Make it engaging for teenagers."

Result: Targeted, age-appropriate, well-structured response.

The Five Elements Framework

Element What It Is Example
TASK Specific action. Use verbs: "write," "analyze," "explain" "Summarize three main causes"
CONTEXT Background information. Who are you? Your situation? "I'm a high school teacher for 9th graders"
FORMAT Structure and tone. Bullets? Paragraphs? Professional or casual? "Bullet points, simple language, one example per cause"
PURPOSE Why asking? Who's the audience? What will they do with this? "Make it engaging for teenagers"
EXAMPLES References or what you're looking for "Similar to TED Talks for students"
‚úì

Not Every Element Every Time

For simple questions like "What's the capital of France?" you don't need all five. Use the framework when you need detailed, specific, or creative responses.

üéØ

Check Your Understanding

1
Multiple Choice: Which element specifies HOW the response should be structured?
‚úì Correct: B) FORMAT

FORMAT controls structure (bullet points, table, paragraph), style, and tone. It tells AI how to present information.

2
True/False: You must include all five elements in every single prompt.
‚úì Correct: False

Simple questions work fine without all five. Use the framework when you need detailed or creative responses‚Äîthat's when structure matters most.

AI's Wild Imagination: Hallucinations & Case Studies

Understand when AI generates false information and analyze real-world consequences

‚è±Ô∏è Estimated time: 11-13 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • LO2.4.1: Define AI hallucinations and explain mechanisms

  • LO2.4.3: Develop verification strategies for critical outputs

What Are AI Hallucinations?

An AI hallucination is when an AI system confidently generates false, fabricated, or nonsensical information as if it were fact. The AI presents it with complete authority and certainty.

Why Hallucinations Happen

  • No Fact-Checking: AI predicts likely text without verifying against reality
  • Training Data Gaps: When data has gaps, AI "fills in" with plausible-sounding fabrications
  • Pattern Completion: AI completes patterns even when accurate information is unavailable
  • No Understanding: AI doesn't understand truth or falsity‚Äîit just predicts patterns
‚ö†Ô∏è

The Confidence Problem

AI expresses false information with absolute confidence. Humans hedge: "I'm not sure, but..." People are more likely to believe authoritative-sounding statements, making AI hallucinations particularly harmful.

Real-World Case Studies

Case Study 1: Lawyers and Fake Legal Citations (2023)

What Happened: Two lawyers used ChatGPT to prepare legal briefs. ChatGPT generated citations to cases that didn't exist. The lawyers submitted them to court.

Discovery: Judge found the citations didn't exist in legal databases.

Consequences: Lawyers fined \$5,000 each. Professional consequences.

Key Lesson: In high-stakes situations, you cannot trust AI without verification.

Case Study 2: Airline Chatbot Misguidance (2024)

What Happened: Passenger asked airline's AI chatbot about bereavement policy. Chatbot explained a refund policy that didn't exist.

Discovery: When passenger tried to claim, they found policy didn't exist.

Consequences: Airline held legally liable for false information from their chatbot.

Key Lesson: Companies are responsible for what their AI systems tell customers.

Case Study 3: Self-Driving Car Vision Errors (2024)

What Happened: Computer vision in autonomous vehicles "hallucinated"‚Äîdetecting phantom vehicles or obstacles that didn't exist.

Discovery: Found through extensive testing and edge case simulation.

Consequences: Unexpected vehicle behavior. Safety risks for passengers and others.

Key Lesson: Vision hallucinations in safety-critical systems can cause accidents.

Case Study 4: Medical Transcription Hallucinations (2025)

What Happened: AI transcription tool "hallucinated" entire medical interactions that never occurred.

Discovery: Medical staff noticed inconsistencies between what happened and what transcript recorded.

Consequences: Patient safety at risk. Incorrect medical records. Potential for medication errors.

Key Lesson: Never rely solely on AI-generated records in healthcare.

‚úì

Verification Strategies

  • ‚úì Check citations‚Äîverify they actually exist
  • ‚úì Cross-reference‚Äîask multiple AI systems the same question
  • ‚úì Be skeptical of specifics‚Äîverify names, dates, numbers
  • ‚úì Consult experts‚Äîfor critical info, verify with humans
  • ‚úì Use AI as draft‚Äîlet AI generate, then humans verify and edit
