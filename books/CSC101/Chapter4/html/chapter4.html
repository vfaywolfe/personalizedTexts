What Large Language Models Really Are

A Large Language Model (LLM) is an AI system trained on billions of words of text to recognize and generate human language patterns. The word "large" refers to its enormous size‚ÄîGPT-4 has over 100 billion parameters (adjustable weights).

üéØ Key Insight: Pattern Prediction, Not Understanding

LLMs don't "understand" language like you do. They predict the most likely next word based on patterns learned during training. This prediction is so sophisticated it can sound remarkably human‚Äîbut it's fundamentally pattern-matching, not reasoning.

How LLMs Generate Text: Three Phases of Training

Phase 1: Initial Training on Massive Text Data

The model reads billions of words from books, websites, and documents. It learns statistical patterns about which words tend to appear together and what sequences are likely. This teaches the model to predict: given some starting text, what word should come next?

Phase 2: Instruction Fine-Tuning

The model is trained on question-and-answer pairs where human experts have written high-quality responses. This teaches it to follow instructions and respond helpfully to direct questions rather than just predicting generic text.

Phase 3: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

Human trainers rate different AI responses, comparing pairs and indicating which is better. The model learns to optimize for responses that humans rate as helpful, harmless, and honest. This is why modern LLMs apologize, ask clarifying questions, and sound conversational.

‚úì

Pro Tip: Understanding RLHF

When you interact with ChatGPT, you're experiencing a model literally trained by humans to be helpful to you. The politeness, the clarifying questions, the conversational tone‚Äîthese aren't accident. They're learned preferences from human trainers.

The Token Prediction Process

When you type a prompt, the LLM generates text through a simple but powerful process:

Step What Happens
1. Tokenization Your prompt is broken into small pieces called tokens (usually words or word fragments)
2. Embedding Each token is converted to numerical data that represents its meaning and relationships
3. Processing The tokens flow through many neural layers that analyze relationships between words
4. Prediction The model calculates probabilities for every possible next token
5. Selection One token is selected (usually the most likely, with some randomness for variety)
6. Repetition Steps 3-5 repeat with the original input plus the new token, generating text one piece at a time
‚ö†Ô∏è

Important: LLMs Have No Access to Facts

LLMs don't check their predictions against reality. They don't have internet access or fact databases. They simply predict likely text based on training patterns. This is why they can confidently generate completely false information (a phenomenon called "hallucination").

Key Terms

Neural Network: A computer system inspired by how brains work, consisting of interconnected layers of artificial "neurons" that process and transform information.
Token: A small unit of text‚Äîusually a word or part of a word. LLMs work one token at a time, generating approximately 100-150 tokens per second on modern hardware.
Parameter: An adjustable value in a neural network that has been learned from training data. More parameters allow learning more patterns, but require more data and compute.
Probability Distribution: The model's calculation of how likely each possible next token is. For example, after "It's raining," "cats" might be 0.0001% probable while "heavily" is 12% probable.

What is Multimodal AI?

A multimodal AI system can process multiple types of input: text, images, audio, and video. Examples include ChatGPT with Vision, Google Gemini, and Claude 3. These systems can answer questions about photos, analyze diagrams, and generate descriptions from visual data.

üí°

Why Multimodal Matters

Multimodal AI dramatically expands what's possible. Instead of just reading text, you can upload a photo, a screenshot, a chart, or a diagram. The AI analyzes the visual information and integrates it with its language capabilities.

How AI "Sees" Images

AI doesn't see images the way you do. When an image is uploaded, here's what happens:

The AI Vision Process
Step 1: Image Conversion ‚Äî The image is converted into numerical data (pixel values and their relationships).
Step 2: Low-Level Features ‚Äî Neural layers detect basic patterns: edges, colors, textures, simple shapes.
Step 3: Mid-Level Features ‚Äî Deeper layers recognize more complex patterns: specific objects, faces, text.
Step 4: High-Level Understanding ‚Äî Even deeper layers connect patterns to concepts: "This is a dog," "This is a beach at sunset."
Step 5: Language Generation ‚Äî The visual understanding is converted to words. The AI generates a description.

Why Prompt Structure Matters

A vague prompt gets vague results. A well-structured prompt gets targeted results. The difference is dramatic. Here's the problem: when you're vague, the AI has to guess what you really want. It will often miss the mark. When you're structured, the AI knows exactly what you need.

Weak vs. Strong Prompt Comparison

Weak Prompt:

"Tell me about climate change"

Problem: AI doesn't know what angle you want, what level of detail, who the audience is, what format you prefer. It defaults to generic.

Strong Prompt:

"I'm a high school science teacher preparing a 15-minute lecture for 9th graders. Summarize the three main causes of climate change in bullet points. Use simple language with one real-world example for each cause. Make it engaging for teenagers."

Result: Targeted, age-appropriate, well-structured response.

The Five Elements Framework

Element What It Is Example
1. TASK The specific action you want. Be clear and actionable. Use verbs: "write," "analyze," "compare," "explain." "Summarize the three main causes" (not "Tell me about")
2. CONTEXT Background information. Who are you? What's your situation? What do you already know? "I'm a high school science teacher preparing a 15-minute lecture for 9th graders"
3. FORMAT How should it be structured? Bullet points? Paragraph? Table? What tone? Professional? Casual? Motivating? "Bullet points, simple language, one real-world example per cause"
4. PURPOSE Why are you asking? Who's the audience? What will they do with this? "Make it engaging for teenagers"
5. EXAMPLES References, similar work, or specific examples. Show what you're looking for. "Similar to TED Talks for students" (optional but powerful)
‚úì

Not Every Element Every Time

For simple questions like "What's the capital of France?" you don't need all five. Use the framework especially when you need detailed, specific, or creative responses.

AI's Wild Imagination: Hallucinations & Case Studies

Understand when AI generates false information and analyze real-world consequences

‚è±Ô∏è Estimated time: 11-13 minutes

What Are AI Hallucinations?

An AI hallucination is when an AI system confidently generates false, fabricated, or nonsensical information as if it were fact. The AI presents it with complete authority and certainty. This is dangerous because people trust the confident tone.

Why Hallucinations Happen

Hallucinations occur because:

  • No Fact-Checking: AI predicts likely text without verifying against reality or databases
  • Training Data Gaps: When training data has gaps, AI "fills in" with plausible-sounding but fabricated information
  • Pattern Completion: AI completes patterns even when accurate information is unavailable
  • No Understanding: AI doesn't truly understand what's true or false‚Äîit just predicts patterns
‚ö†Ô∏è

The Confidence Problem

What makes hallucinations dangerous: AI expresses false information with absolute confidence and authority. Humans typically hedge: "I'm not sure, but..." or "I think..." People are more likely to believe authoritative-sounding statements, making AI hallucinations particularly harmful.

Real-World Case Studies (AIF L7: AI's Wild Imagination)

Case Study 1: Lawyers and Fake Legal Citations (2023)

What Happened:

Two lawyers used ChatGPT to prepare legal briefs. ChatGPT generated citations to legal cases that sounded completely real but didn't actually exist. The lawyers, trusting the confident output, submitted these fabricated citations to court.

Discovery:

The judge attempted to verify the citations in legal databases and found they didn't exist. The citations were entirely hallucinated.

Consequences:

The lawyers were each fined \$5,000 and faced professional consequences. This became a high-profile case demonstrating AI's hallucination problem.

Key Lesson:

In high-stakes situations (legal, medical, financial, academic), you cannot trust AI output without verification. AI can generate convincing-sounding but completely false information, especially for citations and references.

Case Study 2: Airline Chatbot Misguidance (2024)

What Happened:

A passenger asked an airline's AI chatbot about their bereavement policy. The chatbot confidently explained a refund policy that actually didn't exist. The passenger relied on this false information.

Discovery:

When the passenger tried to book or claim the refund, they found the policy didn't exist. The chatbot had hallucinated.

Consequences:

The airline was held legally liable for the false information their chatbot provided. They had to compensate the passenger.

Key Lesson:

Companies are responsible for what their AI systems tell customers, even if the AI was "just hallucinating." Businesses need strong verification protocols.

Case Study 3: Self-Driving Car Vision Errors (2024)

What Happened:

Computer vision systems in autonomous vehicles can "hallucinate"‚Äîdetecting objects that aren't actually there. For example, a system might see a phantom vehicle or obstacle in the road.

Discovery:

Researchers found these errors through extensive testing and simulation of edge cases.

Consequences:

Unexpected vehicle behavior (braking, steering) based on non-existent objects. Safety risks for passengers and others on the road.

Key Lesson:

AI systems used in safety-critical domains must be extensively tested and have multiple fail-safes. Vision hallucinations in self-driving cars can cause accidents.

Case Study 4: Medical Transcription Hallucinations (2025)

What Happened:

An AI transcription tool used in healthcare "hallucinated" entire medical interactions that never occurred. For example, it might claim a doctor discussed treatment options that were never mentioned.

Discovery:

Medical staff noticed inconsistencies between what actually happened in appointments and what the AI transcript recorded.

Consequences:

Patient safety at risk. Incorrect medical records. Potential for medication errors or missed diagnoses based on fabricated medical history.

Key Lesson:

Never rely solely on AI-generated records in safety-critical domains like healthcare. Human verification is essential.
