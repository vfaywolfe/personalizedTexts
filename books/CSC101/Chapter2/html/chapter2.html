Key Concepts and Terms

Computing Innovation: A new or improved computing technology, technique, or application that has a significant impact on society. Examples include the internet, smartphones, social media, cloud computing, and artificial intelligence.
Artificial Intelligence (AI): Computer systems designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. AI systems can learn from data, recognize patterns, understand language, and make decisions based on their training.
Machine Learning: A type of AI where computers learn from examples and data rather than being explicitly programmed with rules. The system improves its performance over time through experience.
Prompt Engineering: The art and science of crafting clear, specific questions or instructions to get better responses from AI systems. More detailed prompts typically result in more accurate and useful answers.
Large Language Model (LLM): An AI system trained on vast amounts of text data to understand and generate human language. Examples include ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini.
Data: Information or facts that AI systems use to learn patterns and make predictions. Data can be numbers, text, images, or any other form of information.
Bias (in AI): Systematic errors or prejudices in AI systems that can lead to unfair or inaccurate results, often arising from biased training data or design choices.
Digital Divide: The gap between people and communities that have access to computing technology and the internet, and those that don't. This creates unequal opportunities and access to information.
Societal Impact: The effect that a technology has on people, communities, and society‚Äîincluding economic, social, cultural, ethical, and environmental effects (both positive and negative).
Ethics in Computing: The principles and values that guide how technology should be developed, deployed, and used responsibly, considering impacts on individuals and society.

Where is Computing Innovation Happening?

Healthcare

AI assists in diagnosing diseases, developing new drugs, managing patient records, and providing personalized treatment plans. Telemedicine connects patients with doctors remotely.

Education

Learning management systems, personalized tutoring AI, and virtual classrooms make education more accessible. AI can help identify students who need extra support.

Business & Economics

AI optimizes supply chains, predicts market trends, automates customer service, and enables data-driven decision making. Blockchain technology enables cryptocurrency and secure transactions.

Environment & Climate

Computing models predict climate change, optimize renewable energy, monitor deforestation, and help design sustainable solutions. Sensors track environmental data in real-time.

Transportation

Autonomous vehicles promise safer roads and reduced emissions. GPS and traffic prediction systems optimize routes and reduce congestion.

Science & Research

AI analyzes massive datasets, simulates complex systems, aids in drug discovery, and helps scientists make new discoveries in physics, biology, and astronomy.

The Dual Nature of Innovation

Computing innovations create tremendous opportunities but also present significant challenges. Consider these questions:

  • Who benefits? Does the innovation help everyone or just some groups?
  • What are the costs? What is lost or disrupted by this innovation?
  • Is it safe? What could go wrong?
  • Is it fair? Does it treat everyone equally?
  • Who decides? Who has power over how this technology is used?

The Basics: Pattern Recognition and Prediction

At their core, AI systems‚Äîespecially language models‚Äîwork through pattern recognition and prediction. Here's a simplified explanation:

How AI Generates Responses
Step 1: Training on Data The AI is trained on millions of text examples from books, websites, and documents. It learns patterns about how words and ideas relate to each other.
Step 2: Understanding Your Prompt When you ask a question, the system analyzes the words and context to understand what you're asking for.
Step 3: Predicting the Next Word Based on patterns learned during training, the system predicts what word should come next. Then it predicts the next word, and the next, building a response one word at a time.
Step 4: Generating a Complete Response This process continues until the AI decides the response is complete, producing an answer that follows patterns from its training data.

Important Limitation: AI Doesn't "Know" Like Humans Do

AI systems don't truly understand. They're sophisticated pattern-matching machines. They can generate text that sounds knowledgeable, confident, and even helpful‚Äîbut they can also generate completely false information with the same confidence. This is called "hallucination." Always verify important information from reliable sources!

Prompt Quality Matters Enormously

The better your prompt (question), the better your response. Vague prompts get vague answers. Detailed prompts that specify context, format, and level of detail get much better responses. This is why "prompt engineering"‚Äîthe art of crafting good prompts‚Äîis becoming a valuable skill.

What Makes Something an Innovation?

Not every new computer program or app is a computing innovation. True innovations typically:

  • Solve a significant problem or meet an important need
  • Change how people do something substantially
  • Have broad impact across multiple groups or domains
  • Create both opportunities and challenges for society
