Key Concepts and Terminology

Computing

The process of using computers and computational thinking to solve problems, process information, and automate tasks. Computing encompasses programming, data analysis, cybersecurity, artificial intelligence, and countless applications across every industry.

Example: When you use Google Maps to find the fastest route, when Netflix recommends a movie, or when a doctor uses an AI system to detect cancer in X-raysâ€”all of these are applications of computing.

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Computer systems designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. AI systems can learn from data, recognize patterns, understand and generate language, see and interpret images, make decisions, and solve problems. AI is not conscious, sentient, or "thinking" in the human senseâ€”it's a sophisticated tool that processes information to make predictions and recommendations.

Example: ChatGPT learns patterns from billions of texts to predict likely next words. An AI camera system learns to recognize faces. A self-driving car learns to interpret road signs and anticipate other vehicles.

Machine Learning

A subset of AI where computer systems learn and improve from experience without being explicitly programmed for every scenario. Instead of giving a computer step-by-step instructions for everything, machine learning systems are trained on data, identify patterns, and apply those patterns to new situations.

Example: Instead of programming a computer with every possible spam email characteristic, you show it 1000 spam emails and 1000 legitimate emails. The system learns the patterns that distinguish them and can then classify new emails.

Algorithm

A step-by-step procedure or set of rules that a computer follows to solve a problem or accomplish a task. Algorithms are the fundamental building blocks of all computing. They describe exactly what a computer should do and in what order to produce a desired result.

Example: A recipe is like an algorithmâ€”it provides step-by-step instructions to create a specific result. A sorting algorithm provides steps to organize numbers from smallest to largest. A social media algorithm provides rules for determining what posts to show you.

Computing Innovation

A computing innovation is a new or improved computing application that has practical value and impacts society. It can be a new software application, a novel way of using existing technology, a new device, or a new approach to solving a problem using computation. Computing innovations have transformed every aspect of modern life.

Examples: Smartphones, social media platforms, online banking, video conferencing, genome sequencing software, autonomous vehicles, fitness trackers, and generative AI systems like ChatGPT are all computing innovations.

Prompt / Prompting

A prompt is an instruction or question you give to an AI system to generate a response. Prompting is the skill of crafting clear, specific instructions to communicate what you want from an AI system. Better prompts typically generate better outputs.

Example: Weak prompt: "Tell me about dogs" Better prompt: "Provide 3 dog breeds suitable for apartment living, with pros and cons for each." The second prompt is more specific about what you want, so the AI can generate a more useful response.

Impact (Social, Economic, Ethical)

The effect or consequence of somethingâ€”in this case, computing innovations. Computing innovations create impacts across multiple dimensions: economic (jobs created or lost, costs, wealth), social (how people interact, equity, access), and ethical (fairness, privacy, consent, autonomy). Understanding impact means recognizing both benefits and harms.

Example: The innovation of social media has positive impacts (connecting distant friends, enabling activism) and negative impacts (mental health concerns, misinformation spread, addiction). Smart analysis recognizes both.

Computational Thinking

A problem-solving approach that uses concepts from computer science. It involves breaking down complex problems into simpler components (decomposition), identifying patterns and principles that apply broadly (abstraction), developing step-by-step solutions (algorithms), and testing and refining those solutions (debugging).

Example: When planning a class schedule, you use computational thinking by: breaking it into constraints (required classes, time preferences), identifying patterns (lab classes need more time), developing a systematic approach, and testing it (does the schedule work? Can you attend all classes?).

How AI Generates Responses

Before you start experimenting, let's understand what's actually happening when you talk to an AI system.

How AI Works: A Simple Model

Step 1: Training Phase (Already Done)
The AI system has been trained on billions of examples of textâ€”books, articles, websites, conversations. During training, it learned patterns about language: which words typically follow other words, how ideas connect, what makes sense in different contexts.
Step 2: Your Prompt
You type a prompt: "Hello" or "Tell me about dogs." The AI system reads your prompt and encodes it into a mathematical representation it can process.
Step 3: Pattern Matching and Prediction
The AI system searches through patterns it learned during training and asks: "What typically comes after this prompt?" Based on billions of examples, it generates the most likely next words, then the most likely words after those, and so on. This is prediction, not reasoning.
Step 4: Output
The AI generates a response word by word, creating something that usually sounds coherent, relevant, and helpfulâ€”but remember, it's based on pattern matching, not true understanding.
Key Insight: AI generates responses by predicting likely patterns from its training data. It doesn't truly understand meaning or have personal knowledgeâ€”it's sophisticated prediction based on patterns.

Important Implication

This means AI can confidently generate information that sounds correct but is actually false. It can plagiarize without knowing it (because it's trained on existing text). It can reflect biases from its training data (because those patterns were encoded). It cannot be fully trusted without verification.

Part 5: Analyzing Impact - A Framework for Thinking Critically

Once you've chosen your topic, the real analytical work begins. How do we analyze the impact of a computing innovation? Simply listing pros and cons isn't enough. We need a systematic framework that considers multiple dimensions: benefits, risks, stakeholders, equity, and future implications.

The Five Dimensions of Impact Analysis

Framework for Analyzing Computing Innovation Impact

Dimension 1: What Problem Does It Solve?

Every computing innovation exists to solve a problem or create new opportunities. Understanding the problem it addresses is crucial. Ask: What was difficult, expensive, or impossible before this innovation? What became possible?

Example (AI Diagnostic Imaging): Before AI, radiologists had to manually examine thousands of medical images. AI speeds up the process and can detect subtle patterns humans might miss.

Dimension 2: Who Benefits and Who Might Be Harmed?

Benefits are rarely distributed equally. Some people gain advantages while others face disadvantages. Ask: Which groups benefit most? Which groups might be harmed? Are there people left out entirely?

Example (AI Diagnostic Imaging): Benefits: Patients get faster, more accurate diagnoses. Healthcare providers reduce costs. Harmed: Radiologists face job displacement or pressure to compete with AI. Left out: Patients in rural areas without AI-equipped hospitals.

Dimension 3: What Are the Unintended Consequences?

Innovations often create unexpected side effects. Ask: What might happen that the creators didn't intend? What systemic changes could occur? What new problems might emerge?

Example (Social Media): Intended: Connecting friends and family. Unintended: Echo chambers, mental health issues, misinformation spread, political polarization, reduced face-to-face interaction.

Dimension 4: What Are the Ethical and Equity Issues?

Computing innovations raise ethical questions about fairness, privacy, autonomy, and consent. Ask: Is this innovation being used fairly? Are certain groups discriminated against? Is privacy protected? Do people have a choice about using it?

Example (Facial Recognition): Ethical issues: Privacy invasion, government surveillance, law enforcement bias, misidentification affecting vulnerable populations, lack of consent.

Dimension 5: What Are the Long-Term Implications?

Computing innovations don't just affect todayâ€”they shape the future. Ask: How might this innovation evolve? What systemic changes might occur over years or decades? What does a world with this innovation look like?

Example (AI and Employment): Long-term: If AI continues to automate jobs faster than new jobs are created, we might need new economic structures, training systems, and social safety nets.

This framework helps you move beyond simple pro/con lists to sophisticated analysis that recognizes complexity.
