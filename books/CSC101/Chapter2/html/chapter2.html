Understanding Computing & AI

Computing and artificial intelligence are no longer abstract concepts confined to tech companies and research labs. They're deeply woven into the fabric of everyday life‚Äîfrom the moment you wake up (alarm, weather app), through your work or school day (email, search engines, learning systems), to your evening entertainment (streaming recommendations, social media).

What is Computing? At its core, computing is the process of using computers and algorithms to solve problems, process information, and create new possibilities. It's about giving instructions to machines and having them execute those instructions automatically.

What is Artificial Intelligence? AI is a subset of computing where machines are designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. This includes learning from examples, recognizing patterns, making predictions, and even being creative.

Key Concepts

‚ÑπÔ∏è Computing Innovation

A computing innovation is the creation or modification of a computing artifact (something built using computing), process, or system that has a significant impact on society. Examples include social media platforms, streaming services, GPS navigation, medical diagnostic tools, and virtual assistants.

üí° Real-World Examples

Healthcare: AI systems that analyze medical images to detect cancer earlier than human doctors alone.

Education: Adaptive learning platforms that personalize the pace and style of instruction for each student.

Transportation: Autonomous vehicles that use computer vision and sensors to navigate safely.

Environment: AI systems that analyze climate data to predict weather patterns and identify environmental threats.

How AI "Thinks"

When you type a message to an AI system like ChatGPT, Claude, or LibreChat, it doesn't think the way humans do. Instead, it uses probability and pattern recognition. The AI has been trained on enormous amounts of text from the internet and learned patterns about which words typically follow other words. When you send a prompt, the AI predicts the most likely next word, then the next word after that, building your response one word at a time.

Think of it like autocomplete on your phone‚Äîbut much more sophisticated! It's learned not just individual word combinations, but also context, meaning, and structure.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: AI Has Limitations

AI systems are powerful but not perfect. They can:

  • Make mistakes or generate false information with confidence
  • Lack real-time information (their knowledge has a cutoff date)
  • Reflect biases present in their training data
  • Misunderstand context or nuanced questions

Always verify important information from reliable sources!

Prompt Engineering: Getting Better Results

The quality of your AI response depends heavily on the quality of your prompt. A vague prompt gets a vague response. A detailed, specific prompt gets a focused, useful response.

Example 0.2.1: Comparing Prompts

Vague Prompt: "Tell me about dogs"

The AI might give a general overview, but it's unclear what you really want to know.

Improved Prompt: "Explain the differences between dog breeds commonly used as service animals (guide dogs, therapy dogs, detection dogs). Include what makes each breed suitable for their role."

This prompt is specific about what you want to know, making it much easier for AI to provide relevant, focused information.

üí° Tips for Better Prompts

  • Be specific: Instead of "write about AI," try "write a 100-word explanation of how machine learning differs from traditional programming"
  • Provide context: Let the AI know who you are and what you need it for ("I'm a college student learning about...")
  • Specify format: Ask for the format you want‚Äîlist, essay, poem, code, etc.
  • Set constraints: Use word count, length, or complexity level to guide the response
  • Refine iteratively: Don't settle for the first response. Ask follow-up questions or ask it to try again differently

AI as Your Research Partner

One of the most powerful uses of AI is as an intelligent research and learning tool. Rather than just providing answers, a good AI interaction is a conversation‚Äîyou ask, it responds, you ask follow-up questions that go deeper, refine your understanding, and eventually develop your own synthesis of the topic.

This is exactly what you did in the previous lesson with your prompt refinement exercises. Now we'll apply that skill to a more substantive question: What really is a computing innovation?

‚ÑπÔ∏è Information Literacy Note

Using AI as a research tool is different from using it as a final authority. Think of it as brainstorming with an intelligent colleague‚Äîit can help you explore ideas, but you should:

  • Verify important facts with authoritative sources
  • Cross-check information from multiple sources
  • Think critically about whether the information makes sense
  • Consider the context and potential biases

A Research Example: Computing Innovations in Healthcare

Example 0.3.1: Iterative AI Research

Initial Prompt (Too Broad):
"Tell me about computing innovations in healthcare."
Result: A very long, general overview
Refined Prompt (More Specific):
"Give me 3 specific examples of computing innovations that have improved healthcare outcomes in the last 10 years. For each, briefly explain what problem it solved."
Result: Focused, actionable information
Follow-Up Prompt (Going Deeper):
"For the AI diagnostic imaging example you mentioned, what makes this a 'computing innovation' rather than just a technology? What problem did it solve that existing solutions couldn't?"
Result: Understanding of what distinguishes innovations from ordinary tools

Verifying AI Information

‚ö†Ô∏è Always Verify Critical Information

AI systems can confidently provide incorrect information. Before using information from AI for important decisions or assignments, verify it through:

  • Academic sources: Peer-reviewed articles, textbooks, course materials
  • Authoritative websites: Government agencies, professional organizations, established news outlets
  • Multiple sources: If multiple reliable sources agree, the information is likely accurate
  • Common sense: Does it make logical sense? Are there any red flags?

Your Final Project: An Overview

Throughout CSC101, you'll be working on a Final Project that explores the impact of a computing innovation on society. This isn't a one-shot assignment‚Äîit's a course-long investigation where you'll:

  • Research a computing innovation you find interesting

The key to a great project: Choose a topic that genuinely interests you! Whether it's related to your major, your hobbies, or something you're curious about, investing in a topic you care about will make the work more engaging and your project stronger.
