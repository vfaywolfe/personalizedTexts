Key Concepts and Terms

Cybersecurity
The practice of protecting computers, networks, and data from unauthorized access, attack, or damage. It includes both technical measures (like firewalls and encryption) and human practices (like strong passwords and awareness).
Personally Identifiable Information (PII)
Any information that can be used to identify, contact, or locate a specific individual. Examples include your name, Social Security number, email address, phone number, home address, date of birth, and financial account numbers.
Phishing
A cyber attack in which attackers send fraudulent emails, texts, or create fake websites to trick people into revealing sensitive information or clicking on malicious links.
Password
A secret string of characters (letters, numbers, symbols) used to authenticate your identity and gain access to accounts, devices, or systems. Strong passwords are long, complex, and unique.
Brute Force Attack
A cyber attack method in which an attacker tries many password combinations until they find the correct one. Longer and more complex passwords make brute force attacks impractical.
DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) Attack
A cyber attack in which attackers overwhelm a website or server with traffic from many sources, making it unavailable to legitimate users.
DNS Spoofing
A cyber attack in which attackers redirect users to a fraudulent website by manipulating the Domain Name System (DNS), which normally translates website names into their true IP addresses.
Encryption
The process of converting information into a code to prevent unauthorized access. Only someone with the decryption key can read the original information.
Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)
A security method that requires two different types of verification to access an account‚Äîtypically something you know (password) and something you have (phone, security key, or authentication app).

Why Cybersecurity Matters

Every time you use the internet, you're creating digital footprints and sharing information. Your email account, social media profiles, online banking, shopping habits, and location data all exist in the digital world. Cybercriminals are constantly trying to access this information for profit, identity theft, or other malicious purposes.

Consider these facts:

  • Data breaches are frequent: Major companies experience security breaches every year, exposing millions of users' personal information.
  • Human error is common: According to security experts, approximately 90% of successful hacks occur because of simple human mistakes‚Äîlike clicking a malicious link or using a weak password.
  • Your data has value: Cybercriminals sell stolen information on the dark web for profit, using it for identity theft, fraud, or other crimes.
  • Attacks are diverse: From phishing emails to massive DDoS attacks, cybercriminals use many different tactics to compromise systems.

Understanding the Threat Landscape

Cyber threats aren't just abstract digital problems‚Äîthey affect real people and organizations:

Real-World Impact

A DDoS attack‚Äîwhere attackers flooded the site with fake traffic‚Äîcaused the outage. While the company recovers, customers' personal information remains at risk.

Your Role in Cybersecurity

The good news? You have significant control over your digital security. While companies and organizations implement technical safeguards, your personal practices are equally important. What is PII?

Personally Identifiable Information (PII) is any information that can directly or indirectly identify you as an individual. If someone has your PII, they can impersonate you, commit fraud, or use your information in other harmful ways.

üí° Hint

Think of PII like keys to your identity. The more keys a criminal has, the more damage they can do.

Types of PII

PII comes in many forms. Here are the main categories:

Direct Identifiers (Most Sensitive):

  • Full Name: Your complete legal name uniquely identifies you
  • Social Security Number (SSN): The most sensitive piece of PII; can be used for identity theft and fraud
  • Date of Birth: Combined with other information, this can identify you and is often used for account verification
  • Driver's License Number: A government-issued ID that uniquely identifies you

Contact Information:

  • Email Address: Often used to access multiple accounts and receive important information
  • Phone Number: Can be used to contact you or verify your identity fraudulently
  • Home Address: Reveals where you live and your physical location
  • IP Address: Identifies your device on the internet

Financial Information:

  • Bank Account Numbers: Enable fraudulent transactions
  • Credit Card Numbers: Can be used to make unauthorized purchases
  • Credit Score: Reveals your financial health and creditworthiness

Online Information:

  • Usernames and Passwords: Grant access to your accounts
  • Browsing History: Reveals your interests, habits, and potentially sensitive searches
  • Social Media Posts: Can contain personal information or be used for social engineering
  • Location Data: Shows where you are at specific times

Why is PII Valuable to Criminals?

Example: Why Criminals Target PII
Identity Theft Scenario: A criminal obtains your name, date of birth, and Social Security number through a data breach. Using this information, they can:
  • Open new credit card accounts in your name
  • Take out loans they never intend to repay
  • File fraudulent tax returns
  • Sell your information to other criminals

The victim may not discover the fraud for months, by which time significant damage has been done.

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning

Criminals don't need all your information at once. They collect PII gradually from different sources and combine it to build a complete profile for targeted attacks.

Lesson 4: Creating and Managing Strong Passwords

Lesson 4 of 10

Why Passwords Matter

Your password is often the only thing standing between a criminal and access to your accounts. A weak password can be cracked in seconds using automated tools, while a strong password can resist attacks for years or longer. Your password protects your email, social media, banking, work accounts, and more.

‚ÑπÔ∏è Info

Attackers use "brute force" attacks, trying many password combinations until they find the correct one. A computer can try billions of combinations per second, so the password's strength directly determines how long it takes to crack.

The Two Factors That Make Passwords Strong

Password strength depends on two primary factors: length and character complexity. Both matter, but research and real-world testing show that length is more important than complexity.

Comparing Password Strength

Scenario: Compare these four passwords using a password strength analyzer:

  • Password 1: rainbows (8 lowercase only) ‚Üí Time to crack: ~2.4 hours
  • Password 2: R4!nB0w5 (8 mixed, complex) ‚Üí Time to crack: ~4.9 hours
  • Password 3: rainbowsinspring (16 lowercase) ‚Üí Time to crack: ~2,000 years
  • Password 4: R4!nB0w5!nSpr!ng (16 mixed, complex) ‚Üí Time to crack: ~100+ million years

Key insight: The 16-character lowercase password is infinitely more secure than the 8-character complex password. Adding length exponentially increases security!

Characteristics of a Strong Password

A strong password:

  • Is at least 12 characters long (ideally 16+) ‚Äî Length is your strongest defense
  • Uses a mix of character types ‚Äî Include uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and special symbols
  • Avoids common patterns ‚Äî Don't use dictionary words, names, or sequential numbers (abc, 123)
  • Is unique ‚Äî Use different passwords for different accounts
  • Isn't based on personal information ‚Äî Avoid birthdates, pet names, addresses, or other info that could be researched
  • Is memorable (or recorded securely) ‚Äî You shouldn't write it on a sticky note, but you shouldn't forget it either
‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: Weak Passwords to Avoid

Never use:

  • 123456, password, qwerty ‚Äî Most common passwords (easily guessed)
  • YourName1990 ‚Äî Based on personal information
  • abc123, letmein ‚Äî Dictionary words or predictable patterns
  • Password123! ‚Äî Too predictable (common pattern with year)
  • Your pet's name, birthdate, or phone number ‚Äî Searchable online

Password Best Practices

Create Strong Passwords:

  • Use a passphrase method: Combine 4+ random words with numbers/symbols: purple-elephant-42-sunset!
  • Use a password manager: Tools like Bitwarden, 1Password, or LastPass generate and securely store complex passwords
  • Never reuse passwords: If one account is compromised, criminals will try that password on your other accounts

Protect Your Passwords:

  • Never share your passwords ‚Äî Not with friends, family, or even IT support
  • Don't write passwords on paper or sticky notes ‚Äî If you must write them, lock them in a secure location
  • Change passwords if compromised ‚Äî If you suspect a data breach, change your password immediately
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) ‚Äî This adds a second layer of security beyond your password
  • Log out of public computers ‚Äî Always sign out before leaving a shared device
üí° Hint

Password managers solve the "too many passwords" problem. They securely store your passwords behind one strong master password, so you only have to remember one password well.

Lesson 6: Common Cyber Attacks and How They Work

Lesson 6 of 10
Learning Objective: Recognize and describe common cyber attacks (phishing, DDoS, DNS spoofing) and understand their mechanisms and impacts.

Overview of Cyber Attacks

Cybercriminals use diverse attack methods to compromise systems and steal information. While each attack differs in technique, they share a common goal: unauthorized access to data or systems. Let's explore the most common attacks you're likely to encounter.

Attack 1: Phishing

Phishing is a social engineering attack where criminals impersonate legitimate organizations to trick you into revealing sensitive information or clicking malicious links.

How Phishing Works
The Attack: You receive an email that appears to be from your bank, with official-looking logos and formatting. The email says: "Unusual activity detected on your account. Click here to verify your identity." You click the link, which takes you to a fake website that looks identical to your bank's. You enter your login credentials and account number. The criminals now have your banking information.

Why It Works: People trust emails from familiar organizations. The fake website looks legitimate, creating a false sense of security.

üí° Hint: Spot Phishing

Red flags: Urgent language ("Act now!"), requests for passwords or PII via email, misspelled email addresses or URLs, generic greetings ("Dear Customer"), links that don't match the displayed text.

Attack 2: DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service)

In a DDoS attack, criminals overwhelm a website or server with traffic from many sources, making it unavailable to legitimate users. The website doesn't get hacked, but it becomes inaccessible.

How DDoS Works
The Attack: A popular e-commerce website is hit by a DDoS attack. Thousands of compromised computers (called "bots") send massive amounts of traffic to the website simultaneously. The servers are overwhelmed and crash. Legitimate customers can't access the site to shop. The company loses thousands of dollars per hour while the attack continues.

Why It Works: Servers have bandwidth limits. Too much traffic exceeds capacity, causing the server to crash or slow to a crawl.

Impact on Your Website: If your website were the target, customers couldn't reach it, your reputation would suffer, and you'd lose revenue during the outage.

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning

DDoS attacks are illegal in most countries, but attackers often demand ransom ("DDoS ransom attacks") to stop. Legitimate companies never DDoS other businesses‚Äîit's a criminal tactic.

Attack 3: DNS Spoofing

DNS spoofing is an attack where criminals intercept or manipulate the Domain Name System to redirect users to fraudulent websites. Normally, DNS translates website names (like google.com) into their actual IP addresses.

How DNS Spoofing Works
The Attack: You want to log into your email account, so you type "mail.google.com" into your browser. Normally, DNS servers redirect you to Google's legitimate servers. But in a DNS spoofing attack, criminals have manipulated DNS records. Your browser is redirected to a fake server controlled by attackers. The fake website looks exactly like the real one. You log in, giving attackers your credentials.

Why It's Dangerous: You think you're on the real website because the URL looks correct, but you're actually on a fake one.

Impact on Your Website: If your Google Site were DNS spoofed, visitors typing your domain might end up on a fraudulent version that steals their information or spreads malware.

‚ÑπÔ∏è Info

DNS spoofing is also called "DNS cache poisoning." Attackers poison the DNS cache (memory) with incorrect information, causing all future requests to be redirected incorrectly.

The Role of Human Error

As mentioned earlier, approximately 90% of successful hacks involve human error. This might include:

  • Clicking suspicious links in emails
  • Opening infected email attachments
  • Falling for social engineering (manipulation tactics)
  • Ignoring security warnings
  • Using weak or reused passwords
  • Leaving computers unlocked or unattended
  • Not installing security updates

Your awareness is your best defense against these attacks.

‚ÑπÔ∏è Info

Your cybersecurity action plan should be personal and realistic. It should address your biggest vulnerabilities and be something you can actually maintain. Perfect security is impossible, but significantly reducing your risk is achievable.

Components of a Strong Action Plan

1. Password Security

  • Audit your current passwords and identify weak ones
  • Create a plan to update weak passwords to strong ones (12+ characters, mixed types)
  • Set up a password manager to securely store and generate passwords
  • Enable two-factor authentication on your most important accounts (email, banking, social media)

2. PII Protection

  • Identify what personal information is most sensitive (Social Security number, banking details, etc.)
  • Limit sharing of PII online‚Äîonly provide it when absolutely necessary
  • Be cautious about what you post on social media that could identify you
  • Check privacy settings on social media and apps to limit data sharing

3. Threat Awareness

  • Recognize and avoid phishing emails (never click suspicious links)
  • Be skeptical of requests for personal information via email or phone
  • Keep software and operating systems updated with security patches
  • Use reliable antivirus/anti-malware software

4. Privacy Management

  • Review app permissions and disable location access for apps that don't need it
  • Turn off background location tracking
  • Limit social media check-ins and avoid broadcasting your location
  • Consider using a VPN for public Wi-Fi networks

5. Regular Maintenance

  • Check account activity regularly for unauthorized access
  • Review privacy settings periodically (apps and websites change them)
  • Update passwords if you suspect compromise
  • Stay informed about cybersecurity best practices

What to Do If You're Compromised

Despite your best efforts, a compromise might still occur. Here's how to respond:

  • Suspected password compromise: Change the password immediately from a different device; check for unauthorized account activity
  • Phishing attack / Clicked suspicious link: Run antivirus software; change relevant passwords; monitor accounts for fraud
  • Data breach notification: Change passwords; monitor credit reports; consider placing a fraud alert or credit freeze
  • Identity theft suspected: Report to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) at IdentityTheft.gov; contact your bank and creditors
